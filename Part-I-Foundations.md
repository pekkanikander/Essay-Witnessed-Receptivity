## Introduction (a draft, to be revised)

Human development depends not only on nourishment and protection, 
but on being seen as a subject.
When such “being seen” is sufficiently stable,
the individual gradually acquires the capacity to receive
without losing a sense of continuity of self.

In this essay, we examine what happens when that developmental function is unreliable.
Specifically, we investigate a recurring pattern:
the collapse of autonomic regulation precisely at the threshold of receiving,
and its wider consequences.

In such configurations, activity, achievement, and even relational performance may remain intact.
A person may appear outwards completely functional in this society.
However, when they experience deeper openness — when attention turns inward and
the individual becomes the recipient rather than the actor —
panic or shutdown emerges.
We argue, towards the end of the essay, that variants of this pattern are culturally
much more widespread than is usually recognised,
and that their consequences extend beyond the individual.

At the individual level, the phenomenon is paradoxical:
improvement in safety and sensation destabilises the system rather than regulates it.
When receiving is unstable, activity becomes the primary regulator.
Due to cultural values, systems organised around performance, ownership, and productivity
often become attractive stabilisers.
Metaphorically, the person attempts to fill internal hollow by outside means.

From the inside, the experience is bewildering.
From the body’s perspective, it is coherent.
From the societal perspective, this ensuing behaviour stabilises the society.

We approach this pattern through multiple lenses:
developmental trauma theory, attachment research, psychodynamic containment,
dissociation studies, and polyvagal modelling.
Each offers part of the mechanism.
Yet mechanisms alone do not explain why receptivity,
rather than action, becomes the privileged site of danger.

Therefore, we introduce a complementary hypothesis:
the capacity to receive safely depends on the early presence of what we call a _witnessing other_.

Our central claim is not that individual mothers are to blame,
nor that this or any other single developmental variable determines adult life.
Rather, we propose that when witnessing of receptivity is fragile — developmentally and culturally —
individual nervous systems may organise defensively around action
while treating receiving as structurally unsafe,
thereby contributing to a number of recurring behavioural patterns observable in contemporary culture.

The essay proceeds from a detailed case study to broader psychological theory,
through mythic and archetypal structures that
encode cultural possibilities for witnessed descent, re-membering, and sovereign receptivity,
and finally to speculation about how this phenomenon contributes back to the structure of our society.
Myths are used here not as evidence, but as maps of relational imagination:
they reveal what a culture can symbolise and therefore stabilise.

We hypothesise that when receiving is structurally unstable at scale,
cultures organise themselves around compensatory logics,
such as performance, control, dominance, productivity,
that stabilise action while marginalising vulnerability.
We do not treat culture as a simple cause of individual trauma,
nor individuals as sole causes of culture, but as mutually reinforcing layers of organisation.
In essence, we tentatively suggest that contemporary Western societies may exhibit such compensatory causal patterns.

This hypothesis does not imply that earlier societies were uniformly more
permissive, egalitarian, or psychologically integrated.
Many premodern cultures were hierarchical, violent, and rigid in their own ways.
Our claim is narrower: that different symbolic orders stabilise different relational configurations.
Some historical and non-Western cultures appear to have encoded more explicit symbols
for witnessed descent, ritualised surrender, or receptive sovereignty.
This does not make them superior,
but structurally different in how receptivity could be imagined and supported.

At stake is are twp questions both intimate and civilisational:

1. What cultural conditions make it possible to receive deeply without loss of self?
2. How does embodied inability to receive deeply affect cultural conditions?

## Background

This essay is an attempt at *cross-domain* sense making:
we approach the phenomenon of (not) *being seen* through several lenses,
none of which is sufficient on its own.
We combine perspectives from physiology, developmental and archetypal psychology,
mythic structures, and societal analysis.

Our working hypotheses are the following:

- **Societal structures** are reflected in individual traumata and vice versa.
  Often this takes place through inter-or trans-generational trauma.
- **Archetypal and mythic structures** describe how cultures symbolise
  (or fail to symbolise) societal patterns, including traumata,
  thereby shaping what becomes liveable, speakable,
  and what remains hidden, unspeakable.
- **Developmental psychology** explains how early conditions
  train physiology into patterns that last to adulthood and often throughout the life.
- **Physiology** constrains what can happen and what cannot happen in the body,
  thereby contributing to the development of the traumata.

We treat these four viewpoints as complementary “maps”, each with its own failure modes:

- Clinical theory only often becomes individualising.
- Trauma language may become reductive and too encompassing.
- Mythic language may remain isolated from physical life phenomena.
- Cultural analysis is often abstract, as tying it to real life phenomena may be too painful.

Our goal is to keep these maps constrained by a concrete emotional and behavioural patterns.
Especially, we focus on how
**collapse of systemic regulation may take place precisely at the threshold of receiving**,
thereby concretely symbolising our cultural tendency of dismissing the value of
vulnerability in creating meaning and connection.

#### Developmental trauma

We use “trauma” in the now-standard clinical sense [^van-der-Kolk]:
not the event, but the _residual organisation of the nervous system and psyche_
when an experience has exceeded then-available resources for integration.
Our primary interest is in **early developmental trauma**:
repeated mismatches in co-regulation and safety,
occurring before the self has stable reflective capacities.
In diagnostic terms, many of the patterns described below fall within the ICD-11 category of
Complex Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (C-PTSD),
which explicitly includes disturbances in self-organisation, affect regulation, and relational capacity.[^IDC11]

From that point of view, trauma formation can be characterised with the following:

- Implicit (procedural) learning dominates early human life.
  When behaviour patterns get installed pre-verbally,
  later insight and willpower often have limited leverage.
- From the system’s point of view, protective adaptations are coherent and necessary.
  Later symptoms, such as numbing, dissociation, hypervigilance, or compulsive behaviour,
  are survival strategies.

We draw mainly on trauma-informed clinical work and developmental psychoanalytic concepts,
using authors such as Gabor Maté[^Mate] and Thomas Hübl[^Hübl] as modern entry points,
while keeping the mechanisms anchored to more classical developmental ideas (holding, containment, attachment) [^Winnicott] [^Bion] [^Ainsworth].

[^Ainsworth: Ainsworth, Mary D. Salter, et al. Patterns of attachment: A psychological study of the strange situation. Psychology press, 2015.]

[^ICD11: World Health Organization. _International Classification of Diseases, 11th Revision (ICD-11)_. 2019.]

#### Polyvagal theory

Polyvagal theory is used here as a _plain-language scaffold_ for autonomic state shifts,
not as a total explanation.  We use the three-stage model (see below) as a scaffold.[^Porges2011]

As we will see, the essay’s phenomena can be described as a _doorway instability_:
when shutdown relaxes and receptive sensation returns,
the system does not reliably stabilise in “safe and connected”,
but instead escalates into alarm, sometimes after a short sampling delay.
Polyvagal language is useful because it keeps the account embodied and
avoids moralising (“resistance”) where the pattern is physiological.

We deliberately avoid overclaiming (e.g., precise inferences from vagal tone)
and stick to clinically observable transitions:
mobilisation, collapse, and the difficulty of remaining relationally present while receiving.[^Dana]

Hence, briefly, at any given moment, the human nervous system tends to operate in one of three broad modes:

1. **Safe and connected**
   The body feels sufficiently safe.
   Breathing is relatively easy, attention is flexible, and it is possible to feel sensations, emotions,
   and relational contact without urgency.
   In this mode, receiving—touch, care, pleasure, attention—can occur without immediate threat.
   (ventral)

2. **Alarmed and mobilised**
   The body detects danger or potential danger. Energy rises quickly. Attention narrows.
   The organism prepares to act: to escape, resist, or otherwise protect itself.
   Subjectively, this is experienced as anxiety, panic, or acute agitation.
   (sympathetic)

3. **Shut down and withdrawn**
   When danger is perceived as overwhelming or inescapable, the system may move in the opposite direction.
   Sensation dulls, emotions flatten, and dissociation or numbness appears.
   This mode prioritises survival by reducing engagement with experience.
   (dorsal)

These modes are not pathologies but adaptive responses.[^Porges2017]
Problems arise when a system becomes _rigidly organised_ around particular transitions between them.

#### Attachment and relational development

Attachment and related developmental theories provide a bridge
between early care conditions and later adult intimacy.
They frame early relationships as the primary regulators of distress and
as the source of _internal working models_
that shape expectations of closeness, safety, and support across the lifespan.[^Bowlby]
We use them mainly to name two structural failures that repeatedly appear across schools:

- **Abandonment**: distress and need occur without sufficient response;
  the system learns “when I open, I am alone”.
- **Invasion / capture**: attention is present but not safe;
  the system learns “when I open, I lose boundaries / I am used”.

Both failures can coexist or alternate, producing later ambiguity around closeness:
the longing for contact and the reflexive threat response to it.
In adult sexuality, this often concentrates around _receiving_,
because receiving entails a partial loosening of agency and boundary control.

In attachment terms, these patterns map onto insecure strategies observed in infancy
and, in their most conflicted form, to disorganised attachment,
where approach and avoidance are activated simultaneously
in relation to the same caregiver.[^Ainsworth] [^MainSolomon]
The resulting internal template can make intimacy feel
simultaneously both urgently needed and inherently unsafe,
which aligns with the essay’s central paradox: panic at the threshold of receiving.

We also draw selectively from Winnicott (holding/false self), Bion (container/contained),
and more generally attachment research (especially disorganised attachment),
treating them as convergent descriptions of relational prerequisites.[^Winnicott] [^Bion]

#### Psychodynamic trauma theory

Psychodynamic trauma theory treats symptoms as organised defensive responses to relational overwhelm,
not simply as maladaptive behaviours.
In its classical form, it emphasises early caregiving failures:
Winnicott’s holding and false self describe how a person can maintain functioning
while protecting a vulnerable core.[^Winnicott]
Bion’s containment model shows how unprocessed experience returns as raw affect or somatic distress
when no relational container was available.[^Bion]
These ideas ground our use of “receptivity” as a developmental function that requires a safe other,
not merely a private capacity.

More recent relational and trauma‑focused psychodynamic work keeps this emphasis on relational conditions,
but makes recognition and the _third_ explicit: without a reliable witnessing other,
_openness is experienced as danger_ rather than nourishment.[^Benjamin]
Contemporary trauma treatment also articulates how defensive organisation can fragment into parts
and how integration depends on relationally supported meaning‑making and co‑consciousness,
bridging psychodynamic language with clinical practice.[^Fisher]

#### Dissociation and parts

This essay uses dissociation language in a restrained way.
“Parts” are treated as a descriptive model for compartmentalised state-dependent experience,
which can range from ordinary role shifts to clinically significant fragmentation.[^Putnam]
A complementary clinical model is structural dissociation, which describes how self-states
become segregated to manage incompatible action systems and affect after chronic trauma.[^HauntedSelf]
Psychodynamic accounts, especially Bromberg, treat dissociation as an adaptive, relationally mediated
partitioning of self-states, bridging to the psychodynamic frame.[^Bromberg]

The key point, for this essay, is not diagnosis but _function_:

- Dissociation can maintain functioning by keeping receptive vulnerability out of awareness.
- When defences soften, previously segregated experience may surface more explicitly,
  sometimes temporarily increasing instability before integration improves.

This is relevant because the essay’s central pattern can look paradoxical from the outside
(“why panic when things get better?”) but is coherent as a shift in defensive organisation.

#### Archetypal psychology

Archetypal psychology is used here to address what mechanism-only accounts tend to miss:
the _forms_ in which experiences are lived and narrated.
In this register, “mother”, “witness”, “devouring”, “abandoning”, “the hero”,
and “the underworld” are not literal persons or events but **recurrent patterns of relation and meaning**.
Hillman’s archetypal psychology insists on deliteralising such images,
not to deny biography but to recognise that the psyche speaks in figures
that cannot be reduced to mechanism alone.[^HillmanMyth]
This is close to Corbin’s notion of the imaginal: images are not mere fantasy,
but a mode of reality through which meaning becomes experienceable.[^Corbin]
Cheetham’s work develops this Hillman–Corbin line, treating imaginal forms
as the medium in which eros, identity, and soul‑making are lived.[^Cheetham]
Tillich’s account of symbol and “ultimate concern” offers a compatible theological framing:
symbols participate in the realities they signify and open depth‑dimensions of experience
that literal description cannot reach.[^Tillich]

We draw on all this to justify archetypal language as a disciplined way of speaking
about meaning‑structures that shape receptivity, shame, and subjecthood,
without claiming them as historical causes or empirical evidence.

Two cautions shape our use:

- Archetypes are not excuses to override biography (“it was the archetype, not the mother”).
- Archetypes are not evidence.
  They are interpretive operators that help articulate structure when ordinary language fails.

Archetypal framing becomes especially relevant where the developmental story intersects
with identity, eros, shame, and cultural scripts—precisely the zones
where modern Western discourse often oscillates between moralism and medicalisation.

#### Mythopoetic sensemaking

Herein we use the term “Myth” neither as primitive science nor as entertainment,
but as **cultural memory of relational structures**: a way cultures preserve, transmit,
and legitimise patterns of meaning that orient lives and relationships.[^Assmann] [^Eliade]
In this sense, myths are not “false stories,” but symbolic frameworks that encode
what a culture can imagine, desire, and endure. [^Geertz]

We treat myths as repositories of:
- what a culture can imagine and legitimise (e.g., re-membering, descent with a witness),
- what it cannot imagine well (e.g., receiving without self-loss),
- and how it frames the costs of those failures.

Importantly, the presence of a mythic symbol within a culture does not necessarily
imply that the corresponding relational structures
were widely or successfully embodied in everyday life.
Myths can encode ideals, compensations, or unresolved tensions just as easily as stable practices.
We therefore read them as structural possibilities rather than as evidence of historically realised harmony.

Methodologically, myths are used as _comparative maps_ of “operators”:

- re-membering labour (gathering the scattered),
- installation of the witness / third term (preventing abandonment or capture),
- sovereignty in receptivity (receiving without becoming an object),
- the “Seth” principle (crisis efficiency and domination as an anti-witnessing logic).

Two methodological anchors guide this use.
Firstly, structural approaches treat myths as patterned transformations that organise meaning
through recurrent oppositions and mediations,
which lets us compare cultures without reducing them to literal events.[^LeviStrauss]
Secondly, modern myth analysis shows how symbolic narratives circulate in everyday cultural scripts,
shaping what feels “natural,” admirable, or shameful in a given time.[^Barthes]

To be clear: we do not claim that myths cause symptoms.
We surmise that myths reveal what is culturally supported versus culturally disallowed,
which matters a lot when an individual is trying to stabilise a new structural and behavioural pattern;
in our case, the pattern of receiving.

### Methods and epistemic stance

This essay is a synthesis built from three kinds of material:

1. **Phenomenology**
   The internal structure of experience as reported and examined over time
  (e.g., numbness lifting → short delay → panic; doing is tolerated, receiving is not).

2. **Mechanistic models**
   Polyvagal state shifts, developmental holding/containment,
  attachment dynamics, dissociation as defensive organisation.

3. **Symbolic/cultural models**
   Archetypal and mythic operators that encode relational structures beyond individual psychology.
