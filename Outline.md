## Background, with Lenses

This essay is an attempt at *cross-domain* sensemaking:
one phenomenon is approached through several lenses, none of which is sufficient on its own.

The working rules are:
- **Physiology** constrains what can happen in the body.
- **Developmental psychology** explains how early conditions train physiology into durable patterns.
- **Archetypal and mythic structures** describe how cultures symbolise (or fail to symbolise) those patterns, thereby shaping what becomes liveable, speakable, and transmissible.

We treat these as complementary “maps”, each with its own failure modes.
Trauma language can become reductive (“everything is trauma”).
Mythic language can become ungrounded (“everything is archetype”).
Clinical theory can become individualising (“it’s only the person”).
Cultural analysis can become abstract (“it’s only patriarchy”).

The goal is to keep all maps constrained by the concrete pattern observed:
**collapse of regulation precisely at the threshold of receiving**.

### Fields of inquiry

#### Trauma and developmental trauma

We use “trauma” in the now-standard technical sense:
not the event, but the *residual organisation of the nervous system and psyche*
when an experience exceeds available resources for integration.
Here the primary interest is **developmental trauma**:
early and repeated mismatches in co-regulation and safety,
occurring before the self has stable reflective capacities.

Key ideas used (without committing to any single school):

- **Protective adaptations** are coherent from the system’s point of view.
  Numbing, dissociation, hypervigilance, and compulsive achievement are not “malfunctions” but survival strategies.
- **State-dependent access**:
  what cannot be held in one state (e.g., ventral safety) reappears in others (alarm or shutdown).
  This matters because the essay’s phenomenon is *state-specific*.
- **Implicit (procedural) learning** dominates early life.
  When a pattern is installed pre-verbally, later insight and willpower often have limited leverage.

We draw mainly on trauma-informed clinical work and developmental psychoanalytic concepts,
using authors such as Gabor Maté and Thomas Hübl as modern entry points,
while keeping the mechanisms anchored to more classical developmental ideas (holding, containment, attachment).

#### Polyvagal theory

Polyvagal theory is used here as a *plain-language scaffold* for autonomic state shifts,
not as a total explanation.

The relevant claims are minimal:

- Human regulation tends to cluster into three broad autonomic organisations:
  1. **Safe and connected** (ventral vagal dominance): social engagement, flexible attention, capacity to feel while staying in relationship.
  2. **Alarmed and mobilised** (sympathetic dominance): anxiety/panic/anger, narrow focus, readiness to act.
  3. **Shut down and withdrawn** (dorsal vagal dominance): numbness, dissociation, collapse, reduced felt-sense.

The essay’s phenomenon can be described as a *doorway instability*:
when shutdown relaxes and receptive sensation returns,
the system does not reliably stabilise in “safe and connected”,
but instead escalates into alarm (sometimes after a short sampling delay).
Polyvagal language is useful because it keeps the account embodied and
avoids moralising (“resistance”) where the pattern is physiological.

We deliberately avoid overclaiming (e.g., precise inferences from vagal tone)
and stick to clinically observable transitions:
mobilisation, collapse, and the difficulty of remaining relationally present while receiving.

At any given moment, the human nervous system tends to operate in one of three broad modes:

1. **Safe and connected**
   The body feels sufficiently safe. Breathing is relatively easy, attention is flexible, and it is possible to feel sensations, emotions, and relational contact without urgency. In this mode, receiving—touch, care, pleasure, attention—can occur without immediate threat. (ventral)

2. **Alarmed and mobilised**
   The body detects danger or potential danger. Energy rises quickly. Attention narrows. The organism prepares to act: to escape, resist, or otherwise protect itself. Subjectively, this is experienced as anxiety, panic, or acute agitation. (sympathetic)

3. **Shut down and withdrawn**
   When danger is perceived as overwhelming or inescapable, the system may move in the opposite direction. Sensation dulls, emotions flatten, and dissociation or numbness appears. This mode prioritises survival by reducing engagement with experience. (dorsal)

These modes are not pathologies but adaptive responses.
Problems arise when a system becomes *rigidly organised* around particular transitions between them.

#### Attachment and relational development

Attachment and related developmental theories provide a bridge between early care conditions and later adult intimacy.
We use them mainly to name two structural failures that repeatedly appear across schools:

- **Abandonment**: distress and need occur without sufficient response;
  the system learns “when I open, I am alone”.
- **Invasion / capture**: attention is present but not safe;
  the system learns “when I open, I lose boundaries / I am used”.

Both failures can coexist or alternate, producing later ambiguity around closeness:
the longing for contact and the reflexive threat response to it.
In adult sexuality, this often concentrates around *receiving*,
because receiving entails a partial loosening of agency and boundary control.

We draw selectively from Winnicott (holding / false self), Bion (container/contained),
and attachment research (especially disorganised attachment),
but treat them as convergent descriptions of relational prerequisites rather than as competing orthodoxies.

#### Dissociation and parts

The essay uses dissociation language in a restrained way.
“Parts” are treated as a descriptive model for compartmentalised state-dependent experience,
which can range from ordinary role shifts to clinically significant fragmentation.
The key point, for this essay, is not diagnosis but *function*:

- Dissociation can maintain functioning by keeping receptive vulnerability out of awareness.
- When defences soften, previously segregated experience may surface more explicitly,
  sometimes temporarily increasing instability before integration improves.

This is relevant because the essay’s central pattern can look paradoxical from the outside
(“why panic when things get better?”) but is coherent as a shift in defensive organisation.

#### Archetypal psychology

Archetypal psychology is used here to address what mechanism-only accounts tend to miss:
the *forms* in which experience is lived and narrated.
In this register, “mother”, “witness”, “devouring”, “abandoning”, “the hero”,
and “the underworld” are not literal persons or events but **recurrent patterns of relation and meaning**.

Two cautions shape our use:

- Archetypes are not excuses to override biography (“it was the archetype, not the mother”).
- Archetypes are not evidence.
  They are interpretive operators that help articulate structure when ordinary language fails.

Archetypal framing becomes especially relevant where the developmental story intersects
with identity, eros, shame, and cultural scripts—precisely the zones
where modern Western discourse often oscillates between moralism and medicalisation.

#### Mythopoetic sensemaking

“Myth” is used neither as primitive science nor as entertainment,
but as **cultural memory of relational structures**.

We treat myths as repositories of:
- what a culture can imagine and legitimise (e.g., re-membering, descent with a witness),
- what it cannot imagine well (e.g., receiving without self-loss),
- and how it frames the costs of those failures.

Methodologically, myths are used as *comparative maps* of “operators”:
- re-membering labour (gathering the scattered),
- installation of the witness / third term (preventing abandonment or capture),
- sovereignty in receptivity (receiving without becoming an object),
- the “Seth” principle (crisis efficiency and domination as an anti-witnessing logic).

This is not a claim that myths cause symptoms.
It is a claim that myths reveal what is culturally supported versus culturally disallowed, which matters when an individual is trying to stabilise a new pattern of receiving.

### Methods and epistemic stance

This essay is a synthesis built from three kinds of material:

1. **Phenomenology**
   The internal structure of experience as reported and examined over time (e.g., numbness lifting → short delay → panic; doing is tolerated, receiving is not).

2. **Mechanistic models**
   Polyvagal state shifts, developmental holding/containment, attachment dynamics, dissociation as defensive organisation.

3. **Symbolic/cultural models**
   Archetypal and mythic operators that encode relational structures beyond individual psychology.

We treat the case history as a **representative compression**:
not a statistical exemplar, but a high-contrast instance that makes a structural pattern easier to see.
The essay’s claims are therefore of two kinds:

- **Strong claims** about the internal logic of the described pattern
  (what triggers what, and why “pushing through” tends to fail).
- **Provisional claims** about culture-level prevalence and cultural blind spots,
  to be either supported with references later or explicitly narrowed.

## A Case study

We present a single, concrete lifeline as a **representative compression** of
the phenomenon discussed in this essay.
Our goal is not to offer a diagnostic exemplar.
Rather, this particular case condenses — within one life history — many features
that seem to appear more diffusely and less visibly across a wider population.

Our goals here are to make the structural pattern visible and to give a
feeling how the present phenomenon may prepresent itself in our western culture.

### Background

The person in question is a now non-binary individual in their early sixties,
born and socialised as a boy and having lived for most of their adult life as a man.
Until their mid-fifties, they fully identified as a straight cisgender man and
did not consider gender identity as personally relevant.
That is, before the onset of their gender crisis at the age of 55,
it never  occured to them that they could be something else as a man.

From early life onwards, work and achievement had became a dominant survival strategy.
Already during school years, pronounced workaholism developed as a way to regulate
internal distress and maintain social functionality.
Alongside this, from adolescence onwards, the person suffered from what was repeatedly labelled as “depression.”
In retrospect, the “depression” appears as having been misrecogninised complex developmental trauma (C-PTSD).

In addition, the case history includes a reported early postnatal pattern
relevant to our later themes of receiving and containment.
According their mother, she had applied
a strict four hour feeding and care schedule for some five weeks after birth,
consistently leaving the baby completely alone until the four hours had passed, "no matter what."
She had told them of having adopted this schedule very strictly,
due to a desire impress her own scoffing and ridiculing mother by being "scientific."
This would imply a prolonged period of minimal contact and limited co-regulation
during the earliest weeks of life.
Indeed, in the 1960s and 1970s, a four hour feeding schedule was recommended in Finland [^1] [^2],
even though it was officially never meant to be as strict as their mother
apparently had taken it.

In their late teens, severe suicidal ideation emerged, including a concrete episode involving
an attempt of killing their mother. (The mother survived without any physical harm.)
This crisis led to approximately six years of psychoanalysis,
followed by more than a decade of various other therapeutic modalities.
Despite long periods of external functionality,
internal suffering persisted in less obvious but chronic forms.

A second major crisis occurred around the age of fifty, marked by renewed and serious suicidal ideation.
This was followed by approximately five years of a depressive-like state with an atypical symptom profile,
severe enough to be seriously life-constraining but elusive to standard diagnostic categories.
At the age of fifty-four, the person made a suicide attempt.
While this attempt did not result in physical injury,
it was followed by a powerful awakening experience and
a radical reorganisation of life circumstances,
including leaving work, home, and long-standing identity structures.

Over the subsequent year, this reorganisation culminated in a shift towards a non-binary gender identity.
Importantly, this shift was not experienced as an adoption of a new role or ideology,
but as a gradual recognition that the previously inhabited gendered self-concept
being no longer coherent with their embodied experience.

### Present symptoms and lived difficulties

At the time most relevant to this essay,
the person’s most salient difficulties manifest in the domain of sexuality and embodied intimacy.
These included:

- Persistent sexual difficulties that could not be explained by physiological dysfunction.
- A long history of genital numbness and dissociation during sexual activity.
- Difficulty experiencing pleasure as something that can be *received*,
  rather than actively produced or controlled.
- Episodes of panic or acute autonomic distress that arose specifically
  when numbness began to lift and receptive sensation increased.
- A broader pattern of difficulty inhabiting the body as a safe place of presence,
  particularly in relational contexts.

Crucially, closer examination revealed that **sex itself was not the primary trigger,**
even though it initially appeared so.
They could sustain sexual activity that emphasised performing, giving, or enduring without acute distress.
Panic and shutdown emerged at moments where the person was invited — or allowed themselves —
towards *receiving*: touch, care, pleasure, attention, or being the focus of another’s attunement.

This distinction is central for our theme:
What appeared on the surface as a sexual problem was, at a deeper level,
a difficulty with receptivity under conditions of closeness and embodiment.

### Discussion

This lifeline makes visible a pattern that is often obscured in less articulated or less extreme forms:
a nervous system that has learned, early on,
that receiving always occurs without sufficient safety, or *witnessing*.
Later in life, this learning expresses itself not as global dysfunction,
but as a highly specific failure mode:
the collapse of regulation precisely at the threshold of receptive openness.

From the experiental point of view,
it is worth noticing that this person had not been able to understand that
they had been experiencing genital numbness or dissociation.
For decades they had thought that everybody experiences sex and touch as they did,
without experiencing pleasure or even any feelings in the penis or pelvic area.
Only once, after their awakening, their numbness started to occasionally lift,
they started to understand that there indeed was numbness and dissociation.
However, after the initial noticing of their condition,
itt still took over four years to really understand the depth of
what they were experiencing.

By tracing this pattern through their single, pretty coherent narrative,
we gain a vantage point from which to examine not only individual trauma dynamics,
but also the broader cultural and mythic structures (and their absences!)
that shape how receptivity, vulnerability, and subjecthood are lived and understood in our society.

## The Body’s Logic

In the case under discussion,
long-standing genital numbness and dissociation can be understood as a *protective adaptation*.
For much of their adult life, shutdown made sexual and relational engagement tolerable
by limiting sensation and thereby reducing vulnerability.

Importantly, this numbness was not global.
The person could function, work, think, relate, and even engage in sexual activity,
provided that the activity remained structured around doing, performing, or enduring,
rather than receiving.

From the body’s point of view, numbness served a clear function:
it prevented entry into a zone where past experience had taught that danger was likely.

### What changed when numbness lifted

Through therapy, relational safety, and other forms of healing,
their shutdown gradually became less dominant.
Sensation began to return.
On the surface, this looked like progress and, in many respects, it was.

However, when numbness lifted, their system was suddenly exposed to exactly what it had been avoiding:
*receptive sensations under conditions of closeness*.

At that point, the nervous system faced a dilemma.
It had not yet learned that receiving can be safe.
So, when sensation increased, the system did not smoothly transition into the safe-and-connected mode,
as happens with neurotypical individuals.
Instead, their system rapidly escalated into alarm.

This escalation did not happen instantaneously.
In the described case, there was typically a **short delay** —
often tens of seconds, sometimes up to a minute or two —
between experienced onset of sensations or attentive closeness and the emergence of panic.
For our analysis, this delay is significant.
It suggests that the system was *sampling* the situation, not reflexively reacting.
When no sufficient signals of safety were detected, alarm took over.

### Why panic appears specifically at the threshold of receiving

The crucial observation is that panic was not triggered by sexual activity as such.
It was triggered by *receiving*: being touched attentively,
being the focus of another’s care or desires,
or allowing sensations to deepen without strict control,
or initially even with control.

From the body’s perspective, receiving involves a partial surrender of agency.
Attention turns inward. Control loosens.
This configuration closely resembles early situations in which openness occurred,
in this case without adequate safety or protection.

In other words, their body was not responding to the present situation alone.
It was responding to a learned pattern: **“When open like this, something bad happens.”**
It is noteworthy that their system had learned this pattern clearly before any sense of self had emerged.
Hence, the triggering did not involve any conscious processes but happened directly at the somatic level.
Experientally, there was no I, no-one to experience, just the plain experience associated with danger.

Panic, in this context, was not a malfunction.
It was the body’s last-resort attempt to prevent a repetition of an earlier, unwitnessed overwhelm.

### Why “pushing through” mostly failed

Given this logic, it becomes clear why attempts
to push through panic, by willpower, technique, or reinterpretation,
were largely counterproductive.

Pushing through easily communicates to the nervous system that:
- its warning signals are being ignored, and
- openness is being demanded without additional safety.

This mirrors the original conditions that led to the defensive pattern in the first place.
As a result, the alarm response tends to intensify rather than resolve.

Hence, What their system appeared to require was not more effort or more surrender,
but a *different condition*:
evidence that receiving can occur
**without loss of self and without being left alone inside the experience**.

From this perspective, panic marks not resistance to intimacy,
but the precise point at which the system refuses to proceed without sufficient safety.

As we will see, it is exactly here where our discussion becomes paradoxically interesting:
we are dealing with a fear for a "loss of self" when there still was no self.

### Summary

Seen through this lens, the body’s behaviour is coherent.
An almost full shutdown protected against danger for decades.
As the shutdown slowly started to relax, alarm steped in to guard the same threshold,
until new conditions had been established.
At the same time, what was happening was fully incomprehensible
to our case person from the inside.
Their experience was simply a panic rushing in from nowhere,
more or less at seemingly random moments,
ruining intimacy and creating a secondary fear towards intimacy.

This all sets the stage for our next question, which cannot be answered by nervous-system language alone:
*what, exactly, constitutes sufficient safety for receiving?*
Addressing that question requires turning to psychological, relational, and ultimately mythic frameworks.

## Psychological Parallels

The pattern observed in our case is not without precedent in psychological theory.
Several strands of psychoanalytic, attachment-based, and
trauma-informed literature describe closely related dynamics.
However, while these frameworks illuminate important *mechanisms*,
they also reveal a persistent limitation:
they rarely connect these mechanisms to broader cultural or mythic structures.
In this section, we situate the present case within established psychological concepts,
while also marking the point at which those concepts cease to be sufficient.

### Winnicott: holding and the survival of the self

Donald Winnicott’s notion of the *holding environment* provides
one of the clearest psychological parallels to the difficulties described here.
In Winnicott’s account, early development depends not only on physical care,
but on an environment that allows the infant’s emerging self to *survive dependence*.
When holding is sufficiently reliable,
the infant can relax into need and gradually develop a sense of continuity of being.

When holding is inconsistent or absent, however, the infant does not simply become distressed.
Instead, more fundamental adaptations arise.
Winnicott famously described the emergence of a *false self*
that maintains external functioning while protecting a more vulnerable core from exposure.

Reading through this lens,
the long-standing capacity of the case person to function, work, relate, and even engage in sexual activity —
as long as these remained organised around doing rather than receiving —
appears as a coherent developmental compromise.
What could not be safely held was not activity, but dependence and receptivity.
The system learned to survive by minimising precisely those states
in which the self would have needed to rely on an external other.

Winnicott’s work thus explains how a person may appear intact
while remaining unable to receive without unbearable feelings of threat.
At the same time, his framework largely remains at the level of early development and individual psychology.
It does not, by itself, address why similar patterns might recur across a culture,
nor how such patterns are collectively symbolised or dismissed.

### Bion: containment and unprocessed experience

Wilfred Bion’s model of *container and contained* offers a complementary perspective.
In this framework, overwhelming sensory and emotional experiences require an external container
to be metabolised into tolerable, thinkable form.
When such containment is absent, experiences remain unprocessed and
return later as raw affect, somatic distress, or fragmentation.

In the case we are studying, one plausible illustration of such early uncontained experience is found
in the reported postnatal care conditions: consistently being left alone until the four hours had passed.
For a sensitive baby, the mere existence outside of the womb may have been overwhelming.
This combined with the almost complete lack of contact with the mother, may have disturbed
the normal development of the self.

The panic responses described in the present case can be understood in this light.
Moments of receptive openness appear to reactivate early states
in which sensation and affect exceeded the available containment.
It seems likely that the moments of closeness with the mother,
taking place on the strict schedule, were simply overwhelming as such.
Importantly, these reactions do not depend on conscious memory or interpretation.
They arise as bodily states that resist integration because
they were never adequately contained in the first place.

Bion’s model helps explain why panic emerges *after* sensation increases
and why it carries a quality of inevitability rather than choice.
However, like Winnicott’s, Bion’s framework remains focused on dyadic psychological processes.
It explains *how* uncontained experience behaves,
but not *why* certain forms of experience are more likely
to remain uncontained in specific cultural contexts.
In particular, it does not explain the prevalence of such experiences and
the role of intergenerational trauma.

### Attachment theory and disorganised receiving

From an attachment-theoretical perspective,
the described pattern bears similarities to what has been termed *disorganised attachment*.
In such configurations, the caregiver is simultaneously a source of safety and of threat,
leaving the developing system without a coherent strategy for seeking comfort or support.

One long-term consequence of such early contradictions is difficulty
with receiving care, closeness, or soothing later in life.
Approaches toward intimacy may activate alarm rather than relief.
This resonates strongly with the present case,
where being the focus of another’s attention or care reliably precipitates autonomic distress.

Attachment theory clarifies how these patterns can persist even in the absence of overt abuse or neglect,
and how they can remain largely invisible until intimacy or dependency becomes unavoidable.
At the same time, attachment theory typically treats these dynamics as individual developmental outcomes.
It does not readily explain why affective, embodied, or sexual forms of receiving become especially charged,
nor why such difficulties often intersect with questions of gender, identity, and spirituality.

### Fragmentation, dissociation, and later diagnosis

The later, post awakening emergence of explicit fragmentation and dissociative phenomena in this case,
including a temporary diagnosis of dissociative identity disorder (DID),
fits within well-established trauma models.
Fragmentation can be understood as a delayed recognition of defensive structures that were always present,
but previously masked by high levels of functional shutdown.

In this case, the DID period involved clear amnesia and time loss,
with multiple distinct “adult” parts lacking access to each other’s memories.
The parts differed in perceived age, gender, voice, and style, and many of them reportedly first became distinct during this period.
The diagnosis was given formally by two independent psychiatrists.

Over time, the most notable improvement reported by the patient was in memory and co-consciousness:
the parts still exist, but there is now markedly enhanced access to each other's memory,
such that dissociation no longer disrupts ordinary life in the same way.
The patient also reports increased ability to relate to parts as parts,
rather than being fully captured by them.
The reasons for the relatively rapid shift from meeting DID criteria
to falling below the diagnostic threshold remain unclear.

From a psychological perspective, however, the broad sequence is not paradoxical:
as defensive shutdown relaxes,
previously segregated aspects of experience may surface more clearly
before new forms of integration and co-ordination become possible.

What remains unexplained, however, is *why* fragmentation and panic
coalesce so precisely around receptive states, and
why these dynamics intersect so strongly with questions of identity and embodiment.
Psychological models describe the *form* of fragmentation,
but they do not fully account for its *meaning* or
its recurrent association with specific relational configurations.

### What psychology explains and what it does not

Taken together, these psychological frameworks offer a robust account of the mechanisms at play:
the protective role of shutdown,
the emergence of panic in the absence of containment,
the difficulty of receiving under conditions of early contradiction, and
the later surfacing of fragmentation as defences relax.

What they do not provide is a language for the broader context
in which these mechanisms acquire their specific shape.
They do not explain why receptivity, rather than activity, becomes the privileged site of danger;
why sexual and embodied receiving are especially charged; or
why similar patterns appear repeatedly at the intersection of trauma, spirituality, and cultural gender norms.

To address these questions, we must move beyond individual psychology alone.
The next sections therefore turn to archetypal and mythic perspectives,
not as alternatives to psychological explanation,
but as attempts to articulate what psychology leaves implicit:
the collective structures
that shape how receiving, vulnerability, and subjecthood are imagined, permitted, or denied.

## Absense of the Witnessing Mother

We have described a pattern: panic emerging from **receiving** — 
receiving under closeness, attention, and embodied sensation.
In the polyvagal language we described this as a threshold
at which the system refuses to proceed without sufficient safety.
From the psychoanalytic and attachment theory point of view we described this
as  containment failure and defensive organisation.

What remains is a more elementary question:

*What is it that was missing, early on, such that receiving become a site of danger?*

We provisionally name that missing element **the witnessing other**.
In early development, most commonly the witnessing other is the mother.
In a way, the term “witnessing mother” is sacred.
It is a functional term: a description of a role that must exist for the infant’s receptivity to develop.
Its absence is not meant morally nor as a claim about individual blame.
Its absence is what just happens.
(We will return to the reasons of the absence later on.)

### What we mean by “witnessing” here

By “witnessing” we do **not** mean any of the following:

- Mere physical presence (a body in the room).
- Supervision or surveillance (“I am watching you”).
- A cognitive stance of interpretation (“I know what this means”).
- Emotional fusion or merger (a parent who collapses into the child).
- A spiritualised observer stance detached from relationship.

Instead, we mean a simple but demanding developmental function:

**Witnessing is the capacity of an other
to stay present, attuned, and non-invading**
while the infant is affected,
**in a manner that allows the infant’s receptivity be,** without becoming annihilating.

In more concrete terms, this witnessing includes three components:

1. **Recognition**
   The infant is registered as a separate being with its own states,
   not merely as an object to be managed.
   The caregiver does not only perform tasks; they track and respond to *someone*.

2. **Containment**
   The caregiver can receive and metabolise the infant’s distress and intensity
   without collapsing, retaliating, freezing, or abandoning.
   The infant’s experience becomes “holdable” because it is held in another mind and body.

3. **Non-violation**
   The caregiver does not demand openness on their own terms.
   Attention does not become intrusion. Contact does not become control.
   The infant is not required to surrender selfhood in order to be cared for.

Summarising, a witnessing caregiver "sees" the infant as an other,
even when the infant's self has not yet developed yet.

These components of the witnessing not ideals.
They are prerequisites for the developmental task that concerns us here:
the formation of a self that can **receive** without disappearing.

### The central claim: receiving without witnessing trains danger

If early receiving happens without sufficient witnessing—whether due to absence, depression, rigid schedules, dissociation, hostility, fear, or simply being overwhelmed—the infant learns a rule. It is not a thought, but a bodily law:

> *When I open, I am alone.*
> *When I need, there is no one to hold it.*
> *When I am affected, I lose continuity.*

In later life, such a rule can remain hidden because it is selectively triggered. A person may function well in domains organised around action, achievement, performance, or control. The rule becomes visible at the precise moment receptivity is required: being comforted, being cared for, being the focus of attention, being touched, being “given to”, and—critically for this essay—being erotically received.

This clarifies why, in our case, “doing” could often proceed, while “receiving” repeatedly activated panic.

### Witnessing as a “third term” that prevents two failures

Developmental and relational literature repeatedly circles a structural point that can be stated plainly: if witnessing is absent, the system tends to fall into one of two failures.

1. **Abandonment**
   Receiving occurs, but without support. The infant’s arousal and need have nowhere to go. Later, openness is avoided because it predicts being alone with intensity.

2. **Invasion / capture**
   Attention is present, but it is not safe. The infant is attended to as an extension, an object, or a problem. Later, receiving becomes dangerous because it predicts loss of boundaries.

In practice, many histories involve mixtures of both. The crucial point is that witnessing is what prevents the dyad collapsing into either abandonment or capture. Witnessing functions as a *third term*—not a third person necessarily, but a third function: the capacity for attuned presence that neither withdraws nor invades.

This “thirdness” will later become relevant in mythic terms, but here it is enough to note that the nervous system appears to require it as a condition for safe receptivity.

### Why we name this “mother”, even when the issue is not “mother”

Calling this “the witnessing mother” may appear culturally and emotionally loaded.
The term can be misunderstood as an accusation or as a narrow claim about mothers.
That is not the intention. We use the word “mother” for two reasons:

1. **Developmental realism**
   In most lifelines, the mother (or primary caregiver in a maternal role) is
   the earliest and most influential context for receiving: feeding, holding, and soothing.

2. **Archetypal realism**
   The “mother” is also a symbolic position that cultures organise:
   how receptivity, dependency, and vulnerability are regulated or denied and thereby shamed.
   (Soon we will argue that cultural narratives about mothering, sacrifice, and gender
   shape what witnessing becomes possible.)

Thus, “mother” here names both a developmental role and a cultural archetype.
Hence, herein the _mother wound_ [REF] is the transmission—across
generations—of difficulties in witnessing receptivity.

### Relation to fragmentation

The relevance of witnessing becomes sharper when we recall the fragmentation of the mind described in our case.
When early experience cannot be integrated the system does not simply remember.
The system segregates, splits into disconnected pieces, some of which seemingly disappear.
Later, when such a shutdown relaxes, what has been segregated can become visible,
first through dissociation, parts, and discontinuities of memory.

From this perspective, fragmentation is not only a symptom.
It is also evidence of how early receiving was handled:
by splitting experience into compartments that could survive only separately,
as they were not being held together by a reliable other.

This does not explain everything about DID, nor does it reduce it to a single cause of DID.
It does, however, align with our central theme:
the integrity of selfhood depends on the conditions under which receptivity was first possible.

-----

At this point we can summarise the situation in one sentence:

> The body demands a witness before it allows deep receiving.

Psychology can describe the mechanism. Polyvagal theory can describe the state transitions. What remains is to articulate how cultures symbolise—or fail to symbolise—this requirement. That is where myth becomes relevant: myths are not evidence for individual causation, but they are repositories of cultural solutions and cultural blind spots.

In the next section, we therefore examine a set of mythic structures in which witnessing is explicit, absent, or contested, and ask what they reveal about receptivity, fragmentation, and the possibility of receiving without loss of self.

## Mythic Structures (outline)

The psychological account above describes *mechanisms* (shutdown, alarm, containment failure, fragmentation).
Myth does something different: it encodes *cultural solutions and blind spots* in narrative form.
Here we use myths neither as proofs nor as diagnostic templates,
but as **maps of relational and existential structure**.

Our working hypothesis is simple: where a culture has stable symbols for **witnessed receptivity**,
the transition into receiving is more likely to be narratable, shareable, and therefore regulatable.
Where such symbols are weak or distorted, the same human dynamics may be lived as private pathology.

The following myths are introduced as **operators**—each highlights one structural element relevant to our case.

### Lemminkäinen’s mother (Kalevala): re-membering without moralising

**Operator:** *re-membering* — the retrieval and reassembly of what has been scattered.

- The son/hero is dismembered and lost to the underworld/river: a vivid image of fragmentation and disappearance.
- The mother does not argue, diagnose, or blame; she **acts to gather**.
- The reassembly is not merely physical. Symbolically, it restores **continuity**: the possibility that the scattered can be held together again.

**Why it matters here:**
- It models the core function we named: *an other who does not turn away* when the vulnerable is lost, numb, or broken.
- It offers a non-patriarchal template of restoration: not conquest, not purity, but persistent retrieval.

**Where we need to reinterpret (for this essay):**
- The myth is explicitly “mother restores son.” Our reading shifts the target: what is re-membered is not “male identity” but **the receiver-self** that had to dissociate to survive.

### Isis and Osiris: reassembly in the shadow of patriarchy (Set, phallus, lettuce)

**Operator:** *repair under a hostile order* — restoration without returning to the old contract.

- Osiris is dismembered and scattered: fragmentation inflicted not only by an enemy but by a political/sexual order that cannot tolerate certain kinds of openness.
- Isis gathers the pieces: again, re-membering as refusal of erasure.
- The missing phallus functions as a symbol that the prior sexual order cannot be simply reinstated.
- Set’s “lettuce incident” (seed without relational consent) becomes a mythic image of **power attempting to reproduce itself without witnessed receptivity**.

**Why it matters here:**
- It brings patriarchy into the frame explicitly: the struggle is not only personal, but structural.
- It supports the essay’s claim that the problem is not “sex” but **what receiving means under an inherited sexual-political contract**.

**Later expansion points:**
- Rewrite Horus as “the Eye” (witness) rather than “the avenger.”
- Clarify Set as the voice of crisis-efficiency: the argument against care.

### Inanna’s descent and Ninshubur: the protocol of witnessed descent

**Operator:** *the covenant / rescue protocol* — descent is permitted because return is guaranteed.

- Inanna prepares a witness (Ninshubur) and gives explicit instructions: if I do not return, you must act.
- The descent is not romanticised surrender; it is a **bounded venture** with an agreed retrieval mechanism.
- The underworld functions as the space where ordinary identity and power do not work.

**Why it matters here:**
- This is the clearest mythic form of “install the witness first.”
- It models a crucial idea for trauma work near thresholds: **openness must be reversible**.

**Later expansion points:**
- Compare “three days” as a temporal boundary to the body’s delayed panic (sampling before alarm).
- Contrast with myths where descent occurs without protocol and ends in annihilation.

### Avalokiteśvara: witnessing as non-intrusive responsiveness

**Operator:** *the hearing field* — witnessing that receives without capture.

- Avalokiteśvara is defined by perceiving suffering (hearing cries) and responding compassionately.
- The emphasis is not on conquering the threat, but on **remaining present to the vulnerable** without invasion.
- This provides a symbolic grammar for witnessing that is neither surveillance nor fusion.

**Why it matters here:**
- It offers a model for “witnessing” that does not automatically become control.
- It helps articulate what the body may require: an attentive presence that does not demand performance or surrender.

### Tārā: sovereign receptivity (agency without conquest)

**Operator:** *the third position* — agency and receptivity without role captivity.

- Tārā functions as a counter-symbol to the patriarchal split: doer/receiver, penetrator/penetrated, ruler/subordinate.
- She embodies responsiveness and protection without requiring “male-coded” domination.
- This becomes a mythic support for a non-binary or post-binary resolution: not as ideology, but as a structural necessity when the old roles collapse.

**Why it matters here:**
- It provides language for **receiving while remaining a subject**.
- It aligns with the essay’s intuition that non-binary emergence in the case is not incidental but connected to the breakdown of the inherited contract around receiving.

### Seth’s Objection (outline)

If the preceding sections describe what the body seems to require (a witness before deep receiving), Seth represents the counter-argument that repeatedly reasserts itself in both psyche and culture. We treat Seth here not as a cartoon villain, but as a **principle**: the voice of crisis, speed, hierarchy, and survival-efficiency.

In this essay, Set’s objection is valuable because it names what a purely “care-centric” story tends to omit: there are real conditions under which witnessing, slowness, and consent-based receptivity are hard to maintain.

#### The core claim (Seth’s stance)

- **Care is too slow.** In danger, hesitation kills; regulation must be immediate.
- **Openness is weakness.** Receiving without control is vulnerability; vulnerability invites harm.
- **Power must reproduce itself.** A system that cannot enforce continuity (lineage, order, dominance) is replaced by one that can.
- **The witness is a luxury.** Witnessing is possible only when basic safety already exists; it cannot be the foundation of order.

In short: Seth argues that witnessed receptivity is not a primary principle, but an *optional refinement* available only after domination has secured the conditions.

#### Why Seth appears precisely at the “receiving threshold”

Seth is most activated where:
- control is loosening,
- dependence becomes real,
- the self risks dissolving,
- and the outcome is not guaranteed.

This matches the case pattern: panic emerges not at “sex” but at the moment receiving becomes possible. Set’s role is to prevent a perceived repeat of unwitnessed overwhelm by forcing a return to mobilisation (alarm) or withdrawal (shutdown).

#### Seth’s two offers (both false, but compelling)

Seth typically offers one of two “solutions”:

1. **Armour** (mobilisation):
   stay in control, perform, dominate, “do” rather than receive.

2. **Stone** (shutdown):
   go numb, disappear, endure, avoid the threshold entirely.

Both preserve survival at the cost of receptivity. Both are experienced, internally, as necessity.

#### The cultural form of Seth (patriarchal contract)

On the cultural level, Set aligns with the inherited sexual-political contract:
- doer / receiver
- penetrator / penetrated
- subject / object

This contract is efficient. It reduces ambiguity. It minimises the need for witnessing by predetermining roles. It is therefore attractive to Set.

Later sections will argue that the cost is severe: the contract makes **receiving** structurally perilous and frequently shame-bound, especially when the receiver insists on remaining a subject.

#### The boundary condition: where Seth is “right”

We will treat Set as a legitimate principle **in crisis domains**:
- acute physical danger,
- warfare,
- catastrophe,
- moments where immediate action is required.

The essay’s claim is not “Set must disappear.” It is narrower and more testable:

> **Set must not govern intimacy.**

In other words, Set has a jurisdiction, but it is the wrong jurisdiction for eros, care, and deep receiving.

----

Seth’s objection clarifies why “witnessed receptivity” is culturally difficult: it competes directly with efficiency, dominance, and role clarity. The next section therefore asks whether modern Western culture has adequate symbols, practices, and stories to sustain witnessed receptivity—or whether it repeatedly defaults to Set’s solutions (armour or stone) at precisely the places where receiving would need to become safe.

### Provisional synthesis (to be tested later)

Across these myths we can already see a shared triad:

- **Re-membering** (Kalevala mother; Isis): restoration of continuity after fragmentation.
- **Protocol / Thirdness** (Ninshubur; the “Eye”): a witness-function that prevents abandonment or capture.
- **Sovereign receptivity** (Avalokiteśvara; Tārā): receiving that remains present and does not dissolve into erasure.

Later sections will:
- compare these operators explicitly,
- show how Set’s objection (efficiency, crisis) contests them,
- and test whether “witnessed receptivity” is genuinely under-symbolised in modern Western cultural scripts.
