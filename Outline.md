## Abstract (placeholder draft, to be rewritten later)

Even relative absence of the _witnessing other_ (the mother)
has long-term consequences for the development of a human being.
Yet, when the mother (or other) is physically present,
if the baby doesn't feel being "seen" as a separate entity,
the experience, when prolonged, may lead to deep trauma.
In this essay (work in progress), we argue that quite a few
people in Western culture may have such trauma, at least in a mild sense,
creating a self-reinforcing structure where
mild trauma-like psychological structures stablise our culture in one hand and
the culture contributes to inter-genrational stability of such structures in the other hand.
In essense, we pave road towards a structural claim that
certain political-symbolic orders and certain trauma-like organisations stabilise each other.

## Introduction (a draft, to be revised)

Human development depends not only on nourishment and protection, but on being seen as a subject.
When such “being seen” is sufficiently stable,
the individual gradually acquires the capacity to receive
without losing a sense of continuity of self.

In this essay, we examine what happens when that developmental function is unreliable.
Specifically, we investigate a recurring pattern:
the collapse of autonomic regulation precisely at the threshold of receiving,
and its wider consequences.

In such configurations, activity, achievement, and even relational performance may remain intact.
A person may appear outwards completely functional in this society.
However, when they experience deeper openness — when attention turns inward and
the individual becomes the recipient rather than the actor —
panic or shutdown emerges.
We argue, towards the end of the essay, that variants of this pattern are culturally
much more widespread than is usually recognised,
and that their consequences extend beyond the individual.

At the individual level, the phenomenon is paradoxical:
improvement in safety and sensation destabilises the system rather than regulates it.
When receiving is unstable, activity becomes the primary regulator.
Due to cultural values, systems organised around performance, ownership, and productivity
often become attractive stabilisers.
Metaphorically, the person attempts to fill internal hollow by outside means.

From the inside, the experience is bewildering.
From the body’s perspective, it is coherent.
From the societal perspective, this ensuing behaviour stabilises the society.

We approach this pattern through multiple lenses:
developmental trauma theory, attachment research, psychodynamic containment,
dissociation studies, and polyvagal modelling.
Each offers part of the mechanism.
Yet mechanisms alone do not explain why receptivity,
rather than action, becomes the privileged site of danger.

Therefore, we introduce a complementary hypothesis:
the capacity to receive safely depends on the early presence of what we call a _witnessing other_.

Our central claim is not that individual mothers are to blame,
nor that this or any other single developmental variable determines adult life.
Rather, we propose that when witnessing of receptivity is fragile — developmentally and culturally —
individual nervous systems may organise defensively around action
while treating receiving as structurally unsafe,
thereby contributing to a number of recurring behavioural patterns observable in contemporary culture.

The essay proceeds from a detailed case study to broader psychological theory,
through mythic and archetypal structures that
encode cultural possibilities for witnessed descent, re-membering, and sovereign receptivity,
and finally to speculation about how this phenomenon contributes back to the structure of our society.
Myths are used here not as evidence, but as maps of relational imagination:
they reveal what a culture can symbolise and therefore stabilise.

We hypothesise that when receiving is structurally unstable at scale,
cultures organise themselves around compensatory logics,
such as performance, control, dominance, productivity,
that stabilise action while marginalising vulnerability.
We do not treat culture as a simple cause of individual trauma,
nor individuals as sole causes of culture, but as mutually reinforcing layers of organisation.
In essence, we tentatively suggest that contemporary Western societies may exhibit such compensatory causal patterns.

This hypothesis does not imply that earlier societies were uniformly more
permissive, egalitarian, or psychologically integrated.
Many premodern cultures were hierarchical, violent, and rigid in their own ways.
Our claim is narrower: that different symbolic orders stabilise different relational configurations.
Some historical and non-Western cultures appear to have encoded more explicit symbols
for witnessed descent, ritualised surrender, or receptive sovereignty.
This does not make them superior,
but structurally different in how receptivity could be imagined and supported.

At stake is are twp questions both intimate and civilisational:

1. What cultural conditions make it possible to receive deeply without loss of self?
2. How does embodied inability to receive deeply affect cultural conditions?

## Background

This essay is an attempt at *cross-domain* sense making:
we approach the phenomenon of (not) *being seen* through several lenses,
none of which is sufficient on its own.
We combine perspectives from physiology, developmental and archetypal psychology,
mythic structures, and societal analysis.

Our working hypotheses are the following:

- **Societal structures** are reflected in individual traumata and vice versa.
  Often this takes place through inter-or trans-generational trauma.
- **Archetypal and mythic structures** describe how cultures symbolise
  (or fail to symbolise) societal patterns, including traumata,
  thereby shaping what becomes liveable, speakable,
  and what remains hidden, unspeakable.
- **Developmental psychology** explains how early conditions
  train physiology into patterns that last to adulthood and often throughout the life.
- **Physiology** constrains what can happen and what cannot happen in the body,
  thereby contributing to the development of the traumata.

We treat these four viewpoints as complementary “maps”, each with its own failure modes:

- Clinical theory only often becomes individualising.
- Trauma language may become reductive and too encompassing.
- Mythic language may remain isolated from physical life phenomena.
- Cultural analysis is often abstract, as tying it to real life phenomena may be too painful.

Our goal is to keep these maps constrained by a concrete emotional and behavioural patterns.
Especially, we focus on how
**collapse of systemic regulation may take place precisely at the threshold of receiving**,
thereby concretely symbolising our cultural tendency of dismissing the value of
vulnerability in creating meaning and connection.

#### Developmental trauma

We use “trauma” in the now-standard clinical sense [^van-der-Kolk]:
not the event, but the _residual organisation of the nervous system and psyche_
when an experience has exceeded then-available resources for integration.
Our primary interest is in **early developmental trauma**:
repeated mismatches in co-regulation and safety,
occurring before the self has stable reflective capacities.
In diagnostic terms, many of the patterns described below fall within the ICD-11 category of
Complex Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (C-PTSD),
which explicitly includes disturbances in self-organisation, affect regulation, and relational capacity.[^IDC11]

From that point of view, trauma formation can be characterised with the following:

- Implicit (procedural) learning dominates early human life.
  When behaviour patterns get installed pre-verbally,
  later insight and willpower often have limited leverage.
- From the system’s point of view, protective adaptations are coherent and necessary.
  Later symptoms, such as numbing, dissociation, hypervigilance, or compulsive behaviour,
  are survival strategies.

We draw mainly on trauma-informed clinical work and developmental psychoanalytic concepts,
using authors such as Gabor Maté[^Mate] and Thomas Hübl[^Hübl] as modern entry points,
while keeping the mechanisms anchored to more classical developmental ideas (holding, containment, attachment) [^Winnicott] [^Bion] [^Ainsworth].

[^Ainsworth: Ainsworth, Mary D. Salter, et al. Patterns of attachment: A psychological study of the strange situation. Psychology press, 2015.]

[^ICD11: World Health Organization. _International Classification of Diseases, 11th Revision (ICD-11)_. 2019.]

[^van-der-Kolk: Van der Kolk, Bessel. "The body keeps the score: Brain, mind, and body in the healing of trauma." _New York_ 3 (2014): 14-211.]


#### Polyvagal theory

Polyvagal theory is used here as a _plain-language scaffold_ for autonomic state shifts,
not as a total explanation.  We use the three-stage model (see below) as a scaffold.[^Porges2011]

As we will see, the essay’s phenomena can be described as a _doorway instability_:
when shutdown relaxes and receptive sensation returns,
the system does not reliably stabilise in “safe and connected”,
but instead escalates into alarm, sometimes after a short sampling delay.
Polyvagal language is useful because it keeps the account embodied and
avoids moralising (“resistance”) where the pattern is physiological.

We deliberately avoid overclaiming (e.g., precise inferences from vagal tone)
and stick to clinically observable transitions:
mobilisation, collapse, and the difficulty of remaining relationally present while receiving.[^Dana]

Hence, briefly, at any given moment, the human nervous system tends to operate in one of three broad modes:

1. **Safe and connected**
   The body feels sufficiently safe.
   Breathing is relatively easy, attention is flexible, and it is possible to feel sensations, emotions,
   and relational contact without urgency.
   In this mode, receiving—touch, care, pleasure, attention—can occur without immediate threat.
   (ventral)

2. **Alarmed and mobilised**
   The body detects danger or potential danger. Energy rises quickly. Attention narrows.
   The organism prepares to act: to escape, resist, or otherwise protect itself.
   Subjectively, this is experienced as anxiety, panic, or acute agitation.
   (sympathetic)

3. **Shut down and withdrawn**
   When danger is perceived as overwhelming or inescapable, the system may move in the opposite direction.
   Sensation dulls, emotions flatten, and dissociation or numbness appears.
   This mode prioritises survival by reducing engagement with experience.
   (dorsal)

These modes are not pathologies but adaptive responses.[^Porges2017]
Problems arise when a system becomes _rigidly organised_ around particular transitions between them.

[^Dana: Dana, Deb. The Polyvagal Theory in Therapy: Engaging the Rhythm of Regulation. Norton, 2018.]

[^Porges2017: Porges, Stephen W. “The polyvagal perspective.” Biological Psychology 74.2 (2007): 116–143.]

#### Attachment and relational development

Attachment and related developmental theories provide a bridge
between early care conditions and later adult intimacy.
They frame early relationships as the primary regulators of distress and
as the source of _internal working models_
that shape expectations of closeness, safety, and support across the lifespan.[^Bowlby]
We use them mainly to name two structural failures that repeatedly appear across schools:

- **Abandonment**: distress and need occur without sufficient response;
  the system learns “when I open, I am alone”.
- **Invasion / capture**: attention is present but not safe;
  the system learns “when I open, I lose boundaries / I am used”.

Both failures can coexist or alternate, producing later ambiguity around closeness:
the longing for contact and the reflexive threat response to it.
In adult sexuality, this often concentrates around _receiving_,
because receiving entails a partial loosening of agency and boundary control.

In attachment terms, these patterns map onto insecure strategies observed in infancy
and, in their most conflicted form, to disorganised attachment,
where approach and avoidance are activated simultaneously
in relation to the same caregiver.[^Ainsworth] [^MainSolomon]
The resulting internal template can make intimacy feel
simultaneously both urgently needed and inherently unsafe,
which aligns with the essay’s central paradox: panic at the threshold of receiving.

We also draw selectively from Winnicott (holding/false self), Bion (container/contained),
and more generally attachment research (especially disorganised attachment),
treating them as convergent descriptions of relational prerequisites.[^Winnicott] [^Bion]

[^MainSolomon: Main, Mary, and Judith Solomon.

#### Psychodynamic trauma theory

Psychodynamic trauma theory treats symptoms as organised defensive responses to relational overwhelm,
not simply as maladaptive behaviours.
In its classical form, it emphasises early caregiving failures:
Winnicott’s holding and false self describe how a person can maintain functioning
while protecting a vulnerable core.[^Winnicott]
Bion’s containment model shows how unprocessed experience returns as raw affect or somatic distress
when no relational container was available.[^Bion]
These ideas ground our use of “receptivity” as a developmental function that requires a safe other,
not merely a private capacity.

More recent relational and trauma‑focused psychodynamic work keeps this emphasis on relational conditions,
but makes recognition and the _third_ explicit: without a reliable witnessing other,
_openness is experienced as danger_ rather than nourishment.[^Benjamin]
Contemporary trauma treatment also articulates how defensive organisation can fragment into parts
and how integration depends on relationally supported meaning‑making and co‑consciousness,
bridging psychodynamic language with clinical practice.[^Fisher]

#### Dissociation and parts

This essay uses dissociation language in a restrained way.
“Parts” are treated as a descriptive model for compartmentalised state-dependent experience,
which can range from ordinary role shifts to clinically significant fragmentation.[^Putnam]
A complementary clinical model is structural dissociation, which describes how self-states
become segregated to manage incompatible action systems and affect after chronic trauma.[^HauntedSelf]
Psychodynamic accounts, especially Bromberg, treat dissociation as an adaptive, relationally mediated
partitioning of self-states, bridging to the psychodynamic frame.[^Bromberg]

The key point, for this essay, is not diagnosis but _function_:

- Dissociation can maintain functioning by keeping receptive vulnerability out of awareness.
- When defences soften, previously segregated experience may surface more explicitly,
  sometimes temporarily increasing instability before integration improves.

This is relevant because the essay’s central pattern can look paradoxical from the outside
(“why panic when things get better?”) but is coherent as a shift in defensive organisation.

[^Putnam: Frank W. Putnam, "The Way We Are: How States of Mind Influence Our Identities,
Personality and Potential for Change", International Psychoanalytic Books, 2016.]

[^HauntedSelf: van der Hart, Onno, Ellert R. S. Nijenhuis, and Kathy Steele.
_The Haunted Self: Structural Dissociation and the Treatment of Chronic Traumatization_. W. W. Norton & Company, 2006.]

[^Bromberg: Bromberg, Philip M. _The Dissociative Mind in Psychoanalysis_. Routledge, 2011.]

#### Archetypal psychology

Archetypal psychology is used here to address what mechanism-only accounts tend to miss:
the _forms_ in which experiences are lived and narrated.
In this register, “mother”, “witness”, “devouring”, “abandoning”, “the hero”,
and “the underworld” are not literal persons or events but **recurrent patterns of relation and meaning**.
Hillman’s archetypal psychology insists on deliteralising such images,
not to deny biography but to recognise that the psyche speaks in figures
that cannot be reduced to mechanism alone.[^HillmanMyth]
This is close to Corbin’s notion of the imaginal: images are not mere fantasy,
but a mode of reality through which meaning becomes experienceable.[^Corbin]
Cheetham’s work develops this Hillman–Corbin line, treating imaginal forms
as the medium in which eros, identity, and soul‑making are lived.[^Cheetham]
Tillich’s account of symbol and “ultimate concern” offers a compatible theological framing:
symbols participate in the realities they signify and open depth‑dimensions of experience
that literal description cannot reach.[^Tillich]

We draw on all this to justify archetypal language as a disciplined way of speaking
about meaning‑structures that shape receptivity, shame, and subjecthood,
without claiming them as historical causes or empirical evidence.

Two cautions shape our use:

- Archetypes are not excuses to override biography (“it was the archetype, not the mother”).
- Archetypes are not evidence.
  They are interpretive operators that help articulate structure when ordinary language fails.

Archetypal framing becomes especially relevant where the developmental story intersects
with identity, eros, shame, and cultural scripts—precisely the zones
where modern Western discourse often oscillates between moralism and medicalisation.

[^Cheetham: Cheetham, Tom. _Imaginal Love: The Meanings of Imagination in Henry Corbin and James Hillman_. Spring Publications, 2015.]

[^Corbin: Corbin, Henry, and Ruth Horine. _Mundus imaginalis, or, the imaginary and the imaginal_. Spring 1972.]

[^Tillich: Tillich, Paul. _Dynamics of Faith_. Harper & Row, 1957.]

#### Mythopoetic sensemaking

Herein we use the term “Myth” neither as primitive science nor as entertainment,
but as **cultural memory of relational structures**: a way cultures preserve, transmit,
and legitimise patterns of meaning that orient lives and relationships.[^Assmann] [^Eliade]
In this sense, myths are not “false stories,” but symbolic frameworks that encode
what a culture can imagine, desire, and endure. [^Geertz]

We treat myths as repositories of:
- what a culture can imagine and legitimise (e.g., re-membering, descent with a witness),
- what it cannot imagine well (e.g., receiving without self-loss),
- and how it frames the costs of those failures.

Importantly, the presence of a mythic symbol within a culture does not necessarily
imply that the corresponding relational structures
were widely or successfully embodied in everyday life.
Myths can encode ideals, compensations, or unresolved tensions just as easily as stable practices.
We therefore read them as structural possibilities rather than as evidence of historically realised harmony.

Methodologically, myths are used as _comparative maps_ of “operators”:

- re-membering labour (gathering the scattered),
- installation of the witness / third term (preventing abandonment or capture),
- sovereignty in receptivity (receiving without becoming an object),
- the “Seth” principle (crisis efficiency and domination as an anti-witnessing logic).

Two methodological anchors guide this use.
Firstly, structural approaches treat myths as patterned transformations that organise meaning
through recurrent oppositions and mediations,
which lets us compare cultures without reducing them to literal events.[^LeviStrauss]
Secondly, modern myth analysis shows how symbolic narratives circulate in everyday cultural scripts,
shaping what feels “natural,” admirable, or shameful in a given time.[^Barthes]

To be clear: we do not claim that myths cause symptoms.
We surmise that myths reveal what is culturally supported versus culturally disallowed,
which matters a lot when an individual is trying to stabilise a new structural and behavioural pattern;
in our case, the pattern of receiving.

[^Assmann: Assmann, Jan. _Religion and Cultural Memory: Ten Studies_. Stanford University Press, 2006.]

[^Geertz: Geertz, Clifford. _The Interpretation of Cultures_. Basic Books, 1973.]

[^Barthes: Barthes, Roland. _Mythologies_. Seuil, 1957.]

[^LeviStrauss: Lévi-Strauss, Claude. _The Raw and the Cooked_. University of Chicago Press, 1969.]

### Methods and epistemic stance

This essay is a synthesis built from three kinds of material:

1. **Phenomenology**
   The internal structure of experience as reported and examined over time
  (e.g., numbness lifting → short delay → panic; doing is tolerated, receiving is not).

2. **Mechanistic models**
   Polyvagal state shifts, developmental holding/containment,
  attachment dynamics, dissociation as defensive organisation.

3. **Symbolic/cultural models**
   Archetypal and mythic operators that encode relational structures beyond individual psychology.

## A Case study

We present a single, concrete lifeline as a **representative compression** of
the phenomenon discussed in this essay.
Our goal is not to offer a diagnostic exemplar.
Rather, this particular case condenses — within one life history — many features

### Case background

The person in question is a now non-binary individual in their early sixties,
born and socialised as a boy and having lived for most of their adult life as a man
quite succesfully, climbing the socioeconomic ladder and fulfilling the societal ideals.
Until their mid-fifties, they fully identified as a straight cisgender man and
did not consider gender identity as personally relevant.
That is, before the onset of their gender crisis at the age of 55,
it never _occured_ to them that they could be something else but a man
or they could do something else but try to make socioeconomic advantage.

From early life onwards, work and achievement had became their dominant survival strategy.
Already during school years, pronounced workaholism developed as a way to regulate
internal distress and maintain social functionality.
Alongside this, from adolescence onwards, the person suffered from what was repeatedly labelled as “depression.”
In retrospect, the “depression” appears as having been misrecogninised complex developmental trauma (C-PTSD).

In addition, the case history includes a reported early postnatal pattern
relevant to our later themes of receiving and containment.
According to their mother, she had applied
a strict four hour feeding and care schedule for some five weeks after birth,
consistently leaving the baby completely alone until the four hours had passed, "no matter what."
She told of having adopted this schedule very strictly,
due to a desire impress her own scoffing and ridiculing mother by being "scientific."

This would imply a prolonged period of minimal contact and limited co-regulation
during the earliest weeks of life.
Indeed, in the 1960s and 1970s,
a four hour feeding schedule was recommended in Finland[^Rainto] [^Sinervo],
even though it was officially never meant to be as strict as their mother
apparently had taken it.

[^Rainto: Rainto, S., Ihme, A. Imetystä eri vuosikymmenten aikana. ePooki 73/2019.
https://www.theseus.fi/bitstream/handle/10024/262177/ePooki%2073_2019.pdf]

[^Sinervo: Sinervo, A. Lastenhoito-opas. Maatalousnaisten keskus.]

In their late teens, severe suicidal ideation emerged, including a concrete episode involving
an attempt of killing their mother. (The mother survived without any physical harm.)
This crisis led to approximately six years of psychoanalysis,
followed by more than a decade of various other therapeutic modalities.
Despite long periods of external functionality,
internal suffering persisted in less obvious but chronic forms.

A second major crisis occurred around the age of fifty, marked by renewed and serious suicidal ideation.
This was followed by approximately five years of a depressive-like state with an atypical symptom profile,
severe enough to be seriously life-constraining but elusive to standard diagnostic categories.
At the age of fifty-four, the person made a suicide attempt.
While this attempt did not result in physical injury,
it was followed by a powerful awakening experience and
a radical reorganisation of life circumstances,
including leaving work, home, and long-standing identity structures.

Over the subsequent year, this reorganisation culminated in a shift towards a non-binary gender identity.
Importantly, this shift was not experienced as an adoption of a new role or ideology,
but as a gradual recognition that the previously inhabited gendered self-concept
being no longer coherent with their embodied experience.

Improtantly for our later societal discussion, between the first and second major crises,
our case person lived an outwartedly dream life.

From the societal point of view, they became a highly successful member of the society.
They were a serial entrepreneur and university professor,
arguably among the top 200 researchers in their worldwide field at their primetime.
They co-raised several millions both for their startups and their research group.
They found a family, raised children and remained faithful for over three decades.

Hence, from the outside point of view, they did everything right.
They became a model individual, climbing the social ladder.
They were a good son fulfilling the dreams of their parents,
while, all the time, being deeply unsatisfied and depressed.

Only after their awakening they report starting to understand life and its meaning.
At the same time, they became a societal outcast: a psychiatric patient, jobless, and at times homeless.
A happy and meaningful outcast, as they framed it.

### Highlighted symptoms and lived difficulties

When the period most relevant to this essay started,
the person’s most salient difficulties manifested in the domain of sexuality and embodied intimacy.
These included:

- Persistent sexual difficulties that could not be explained by physiological dysfunction.
- A lifelong history of genital numbness and dissociation during sexual activity.
- Difficulty experiencing pleasure as something that can be _received_,
  rather than actively produced or controlled.
- Episodes of panic or acute autonomic distress that arose specifically
  when numbness began to lift and receptive sensation increased.
- A broader pattern of difficulty inhabiting the body as a safe place of presence,
  particularly in relational contexts.

In particular, they initially started to experience inexplainable panic attacks at various situations,
including those involving even the _possibility_ of intimacy or touch.
The first panic attacks lasted for hours.
From that the panic spread to encompassing most fields of social activity,
making the person to isolate themself for several months,
during which the frequency of panic attacks increased while their length and intensity gradually decreased.

In about two years from the first panic attacks, they had mostly disappeared in everyday life,
condensing into two distinctive modalities.
First, receiving unexpected touch, such as someone touching them without them being prepared for it,
basically always triggered some level of a startling or panicking reaction.
This reaction had apparently been there for years in an largely unnoticed form.
Second, the person's system started to develop panic attacks while being engaged in sexual activity.
The person themself initially considered these as completely separate.

At the time when panics attacks were daily or weekly,
the person's dissociation grew to the level where he received a DID diagnosis.
The diagnosis was given formally by two independent psychiatrists.
The DID period involved clear amnesia and time loss,
with multiple distinct “adult” parts lacking access to each other’s memories.
The parts differed in perceived age, gender, voice, and style,
and many of them reportedly first became distinct during this period.
Somewhat unusually, the worst dissociative symptoms started to disappear quite soon,
in less than a year from the person receiving the diagnosis.
The reasons for this relatively rapid shift from meeting DID criteria
to falling below the diagnostic threshold remain unclear.

Now, crucially for our discussion, closer examination revealed
that **sex itself was not the actual trigger** for the later panic attacks,
even though it initially appeared so.
They could sustain sexual activity that emphasised performing, giving, or enduring without acute distress.
Panic and shutdown emerged at moments where the person was invited — or allowed themselves —
towards _receiving_: touch, care, pleasure, attention, or being the focus of another’s attunement.
In all cases the triggering affect was receiving attentive touch and care.

At the pivotal point, when the person's integration started to really take place,
they found in themself a clearly separate part
that experienced horror from them just planning
to meet their partner or some other person with whom there might be intimacy.
To this part the mere potentiality of becoming attentively touched was so terrifying
that it caused a deep bodily alarm reaction,
which required using intensive yogic breathing techniques for several minutes
in order for the body to calm down.

### Discussion

The distinction between acting and receiving is central for our theme:
What appeared on the surface as a sexual problem was, at a deeper level,
a difficulty with receptivity under conditions of closeness and embodiment.

Our example person's lifeline makes visible this pattern
that is often obscured in less articulated or less extreme forms:
a nervous system that has learned, early on,
that receiving always occurs without sufficient safety, or _witnessing_.
Later in life, this learning expresses itself not as global dysfunction,
but as a highly specific failure mode:
the collapse of regulation precisely at the threshold of receptive openness,
which in their case led to decades of subconsciously avoiding intimacy and openness.

From the experiental point of view,
it is worth emphasising that this person had not been able to understand that
they had been experiencing genital numbness, dissociation and
active but subsconscious avoiding of intimacy.
For decades they had thought that everybody experiences sex and intimacy as they did,
without experiencing pleasure or even any feelings in the penis or pelvic area.
Only once their numbness started to occasionally lift, after their awakening experience,
they started to understand that there indeed was numbness, dissociation, and avoidance.
However, after the initial noticing of their condition,
it still took over four years to really understand the depth of
what they were experiencing.
(At this writing, their healing from numbness, dissociation, and social avoidance is still ongoing.)

By tracing this pattern through their single, pretty coherent narrative,
we gain a vantage point from which to examine not only individual trauma dynamics,
but also the broader cultural and mythic structures (and their absences!)
that shape how receptivity, vulnerability, and subjecthood are lived and understood in our society,
and how this phenomenon both shapes our society and our society maintains this phenomenon.

## The Body’s Logic

In the case under discussion,
long-standing genital numbness and dissociation can be understood as a _protective adaptation_.
For much of their adult life, avoidance and shutdown made sexual and relational engagement tolerable
by limiting sensation and thereby reducing vulnerability.

Importantly, their numbness was not global.
The person could function, work, think, relate, and even engage in sexual activity,
provided that the activity remained structured around doing, performing, or enduring,
rather than receiving.

From the body’s point of view, numbness served a clear function:
it prevented entry into a zone where past experience had taught that danger was likely.

### What changed when numbness lifted

Through therapy, relational safety, and other forms of healing,
their shutdown gradually became less dominant.
Sensation began to return.
On the surface, this looked like progress and, in many respects, it was.

However, when numbness lifted, their system was suddenly exposed to exactly what it had been avoiding:
_receptive sensations under conditions of closeness_.

At that point, the nervous system faced a dilemma.
It had not yet learned that receiving can be safe.
So, when sensation increased, the system did not smoothly transition into the safe-and-connected mode,
as happens with neurotypical individuals.
Instead, their system rapidly escalated into alarm.

This escalation did not happen instantaneously: its onset varied.
Initially, the mere thought of intimacy triggered a panic.
Later, when the healing had progressed, there was typically a **short delay** —
often tens of seconds, sometimes up to a minute or two —
between experienced onset of sensations or attentive closeness and the emergence of panic.

For our analysis, this delay and its development is significant.
It suggests that the system was _sampling_ the situation, not reflexively reacting.
When no sufficient signals of safety were detected, alarm took over.
Initially, mere imagined or desired receptive intimacy was enough to raise the alarm.
Later on, with healing, receptive intimacy was tolerated, but only in very slowly increasing doses.

### Why panic appears specifically at the threshold of receiving

The crucial observation is that panic was not triggered by sexual activity as such.
It was triggered by _receiving_: being touched attentively,
being the focus of another’s care or desires,
or allowing sensations to deepen without strict control,
or initially even with control.

From the body’s perspective, receiving involves a partial surrender of agency.
Attention turns inward. Control loosens.
This configuration closely resembles early situations in which openness occurred,
in this case without adequate safety or protection.

In other words, their body was not responding to the present situation alone.
It was responding to a learned pattern: **“When open like this, something bad happens.”**
It is noteworthy that their system had learned this pattern clearly before any sense of self had emerged.
Hence, the triggering did not involve any conscious processes but happened directly at the somatic level.
Experientally, there was no I, no-one to experience, just the plain experience associated with danger.

It is important to note that the same individual tolerates autonomic intensity in achievement,
crisis, and performance contexts, including high-intensity but performative sex.
High arousal during work, competition, intellectual challenge, external emergencies,
or even sex when performed, did not precipitate comparable panic or fragmentation.
Indeed, their ability to function in emergency situations appears to be well above the average.
This strongly suggests that the trigger is not autonomic intensity per se,
but specifically relationally receptive intensity —
arousal that arises while loosening control and the body begins to be affected by another’s presence.

Panic, in this context, was not a malfunction.
It was the body’s last-resort attempt to prevent a repetition of an earlier, unwitnessed overwhelm.

### Why “pushing through” mostly failed

Given this logic, it becomes clear why their earlier attempts
to push through panic, by willpower, technique, or reinterpretation,
were largely counterproductive.

Pushing through easily communicates to the nervous system that:

- its warning signals are being ignored, and
- openness is being demanded without additional safety.

This mirrors the original conditions that led to the defensive pattern in the first place.
As a result, the alarm response tends to intensify rather than resolve.

Hence, what their system appeared to require was not more effort or more surrender,
but a _different condition_: evidence that
**receiving can occur without loss of self and without being left alone inside the experience.**

From this perspective, panic marks not resistance to intimacy,
but the precise point at which the system refuses to proceed without sufficient safety.

As we will see, it is exactly here where our discussion becomes paradoxically interesting:
we are dealing with a fear for a "loss of self" when there still was no self.

### Summary

Seen through this lens, the body’s behaviour is coherent for our case person.
An almost full shutdown protected against danger for decades.
As the shutdown slowly started to relax, alarm steped in to guard the same threshold,
until new conditions had been established.
At the same time, what was happening was fully incomprehensible
to our case person from the inside.
Their experience was simply a panic rushing in from nowhere,
more or less at seemingly random moments,
ruining intimacy and creating a secondary fear towards intimacy.

This all sets the stage for our next question, which cannot be answered by nervous-system language alone:
_what, exactly, constitutes sufficient safety for receiving?_
Addressing that question requires turning to psychological, relational, and ultimately mythic frameworks.

## Psychological Parallels

The pattern observed in our case is not without precedent in psychological theory.
Several strands of psychoanalytic, attachment-based, and
trauma-informed literature describe closely related dynamics.
However, while these frameworks illuminate important _mechanisms_,
they also reveal a persistent limitation:
they rarely connect these mechanisms to broader cultural or mythic structures.
In this section, we situate the present case within established psychological concepts,
while also marking the point at which those concepts cease to be sufficient.

### Winnicott: holding and the survival of the self

Donald Winnicott’s notion of the *holding environment* provides
one of the clearest psychological parallels to the difficulties described here.
In Winnicott’s account, early development depends not only on physical care,
but on an environment that allows the infant’s emerging self to *survive dependence*.
When holding is sufficiently reliable,
the infant can relax into need and gradually develop a sense of continuity of being.

When holding is inconsistent or absent, however, the infant does not simply become distressed.
Instead, more fundamental adaptations arise.
Winnicott famously described the emergence of a *false self*
that maintains external functioning while protecting a more vulnerable core from exposure.

Reading through this lens,
the long-standing capacity of the case person to function, work, relate, and even engage in sexual activity —
as long as these remained organised around doing rather than receiving —
appears as a coherent developmental compromise.
What could not be safely held was not activity, but dependence and receptivity.
The system learned to survive by minimising precisely those states
in which the self would have needed to rely on an external other.

Winnicott’s work thus explains how a person may appear intact
while remaining unable to receive without unbearable feelings of threat.
At the same time, his framework largely remains at the level of early development and individual psychology.
It does not, by itself, address why similar patterns might recur across a culture,
nor how such patterns are collectively symbolised or dismissed.

### Bion: containment and unprocessed experience

Wilfred Bion’s model of _container and contained_ offers a complementary perspective.
In this framework, overwhelming sensory and emotional experiences require an external container
to be metabolised into tolerable, thinkable form.
When such containment is absent, experiences remain unprocessed and
return later as raw affect, somatic distress, or fragmentation.

In the case we are studying, one plausible illustration of such early uncontained experience is found
in the reported postnatal care conditions: consistently being left alone until the four hours had passed.
For a sensitive baby, the mere existence outside of the womb may have been overwhelming.
This combined with the almost complete lack of contact with the mother may have disturbed
the normal development of the self.

The panic responses described in the present case can be understood in this light.
Moments of receptive openness appear to reactivate early states
in which sensation and affect exceeded the available containment.
It seems likely that the moments of closeness with the mother,
taking place on the strict schedule, were simply overwhelming as such.
Importantly, these reactions do not depend on conscious memory or interpretation.
They arise as bodily states that resist integration because
they were never adequately contained in the first place.

Bion’s model helps explain why panic emerges _after_ sensation increases
and why it carries a quality of inevitability rather than choice.
However, like Winnicott’s, Bion’s framework remains focused on dyadic psychological processes.
It explains _how_ uncontained experience behaves,
but not _why_ certain forms of experience are more likely
to remain uncontained in specific cultural contexts.
In particular, it does not explain the prevalence of such experiences and
the role of intergenerational trauma.

### Attachment theory and disorganised receiving

From an attachment-theoretical perspective,
the described pattern bears similarities to what has been termed _disorganised attachment_.
In such configurations, the caregiver is simultaneously a source of safety and of threat,
leaving the developing system without a coherent strategy for seeking comfort or support.

One long-term consequence of such early contradictions is difficulty
with receiving care, closeness, or soothing later in life.
Approaches toward intimacy may activate alarm rather than relief.
This resonates strongly with the present case,
where being the focus of another’s attention or care reliably precipitates autonomic distress.

Attachment theory clarifies how these patterns can persist even in the absence of overt abuse or neglect,
and how they can remain largely invisible until receptive intimacy or dependency becomes unavoidable.
At the same time, attachment theory typically treats these dynamics as individual developmental outcomes.
It does not readily explain why affective, embodied, or sexual forms of receiving become especially charged,
nor why such difficulties often intersect with questions of gender, identity, and spirituality.

### Fragmentation, dissociation, and later diagnosis

The later, post awakening emergence of explicit fragmentation and dissociative phenomena in this case,
including the temporary diagnosis of dissociative identity disorder (DID),
fits within well-established trauma models.
Fragmentation can be understood as a delayed recognition of defensive structures that were always present,
but previously masked by high levels of functional shutdown.

Over time, the most notable improvement reported by the patient was in memory and co-consciousness:
the parts still exist, but there is now markedly enhanced access to each other's memory,
such that dissociation no longer disrupts ordinary life in the same way.
The patient also reports increased ability to relate to parts as parts,
rather than being fully captured by them.

From a psychological perspective, however, the broad sequence is not paradoxical:
as defensive shutdown relaxes,
previously segregated aspects of experience may surface more clearly
before new forms of integration and co-ordination become possible.

What remains unexplained, however, is _why_ fragmentation and panic
coalesce so precisely around receptive states, and
why these dynamics intersect so strongly with questions of identity and embodiment.
Psychological models describe the _form_ of fragmentation,
but they do not fully account for its _meaning_ or
its recurrent association with specific relational configurations.

### What psychology explains and what it does not

Taken together, these psychological frameworks offer a robust account of the mechanisms at play:
the protective role of shutdown,
the emergence of panic in the absence of containment,
the difficulty of receiving under conditions of early contradiction, and
the later surfacing of fragmentation as defences relax.

What they do not provide is a language for the broader context
in which these mechanisms acquire their specific shape.
They do not explain why receptivity, rather than activity, becomes the privileged site of danger;
why sexual and embodied receiving are especially charged; or
why similar patterns appear repeatedly at the intersection of trauma, spirituality, and cultural gender norms.

To address these questions, we must move beyond individual psychology alone.
The next sections therefore turn to archetypal and mythic perspectives,
not as alternatives to psychological explanation,
but as attempts to articulate what psychology leaves implicit:
the collective structures
that shape how receiving, vulnerability, and subjecthood are imagined, permitted, or denied.

### Merely engulfment, humiliation, or shame?

A reasonable objection to our narrative is
that the present case pattern could be reduced to more familiar trauma configurations:
humiliation, disgust conditioning, moral prohibition, or fear of engulfment.
These frameworks are indeed clinically relevant and may coexist with the dynamics described here.
However, they presuppose a subject capable of remaining intact while being judged, invaded, or condemned.
In the case examined, panic emerged prior to evaluative content,
in the absence of narrative shame, and even in the presence of a trusted partner.
The triggering configuration was not exposure to judgement nor threat of domination,
but the rise of receptive sensation itself.
This suggests a more primary instability:
difficulty maintaining continuity of being when affect intensifies.
Engulfment concerns the loss of boundaries; humiliation concerns the loss of status;
disgust concerns contamination; moral imprint concerns transgression.
The pattern described here concerns the collapse of continuity at the threshold of openness.
It may therefore function as a precondition upon which other trauma grammars later build.

Phenomenologically, this layer often becomes visible only after more recognisable configurations
(boundary fear, shame, disgust) have been partly metabolised,
because those configurations can function as surface explanations and defences.
For example, our case person had previously worked through themes
consistent with engulfment anxiety and sexual shame/disgust conditioning.

Nevertheless, the present pattern cannot be reduced to them:
its signature is that panic arises prior to evaluative content,
without a judging gaze, and even with a trusted partner, specifically at the onset of receptive sensation.
This suggests a different organising threat: not “I will be judged” or “I will be invaded”,
but “when I open, continuity collapses”.

At the same time, we do not claim universality:
many individuals with humiliation or engulfment trauma do not show this specific receptive-threshold panic.
Our claim is narrower: in the pattern described here,
continuity-collapse at receptivity appears developmentally prior and
can serve as a substrate on which other trauma grammars later build.

## Absence of the Witnessing Mother

We have described a pattern: panic emerging from **receiving** — 
receiving under closeness, attention, and embodied sensation.
In the polyvagal language we described this as a threshold
at which the system refuses to proceed without sufficient safety.
From the psychoanalytic and attachment theory point of view we described this
as containment failure and defensive organisation.

What remains is a more elementary question:

_What is it that was missing, early on, such that receiving become a site of danger?_

We provisionally name that missing element **the witnessing other**.
In early development, most commonly the witnessing other is the mother.
In a way, the term “witnessing mother” is sacred.
It is a functional term: a description of a role that must exist for the infant’s receptivity to develop.
Its absence is not meant morally nor as a claim about individual blame.
Its absence is what just happens.
(We will return to the reasons of the absence later on.)

### What we mean by “witnessing” here

By “witnessing” we do **not** mean any of the following:

- Mere physical presence (a body in the room).
- Supervision or surveillance (“I am watching you”).
- A cognitive stance of interpretation (“I know what this means”).
- Emotional fusion or merger (a parent who collapses into the child).
- A spiritualised observer stance detached from relationship.

Instead, we mean a simple but demanding developmental function:

**Witnessing is the capacity of an other to stay present, attuned, and non-invading**
while the infant is affected,
**in a manner that allows the infant’s receptivity be,** without becoming annihilating.

In more concrete terms, this witnessing includes three components:

1. **Recognition**
   The infant is registered as a _separate being_ with its own states,
   not merely as an object to be managed.
   The caregiver does not only perform tasks; they track and respond to _someone_.

2. **Containment**
   The caregiver can receive and metabolise the infant’s distress and intensity
   without collapsing, retaliating, freezing, or abandoning.
   The infant’s experience becomes “holdable” because it is held in another mind and body.

3. **Non-violation**
   The caregiver does not demand openness on their own terms.
   Attention does not become intrusion. Contact does not become control.
   The infant is not required to surrender selfhood in order to be cared for.

Summarising, a witnessing caregiver "sees" the infant as an other,
even when the infant's self has not yet developed yet.

These components of the witnessing not ideals.
They are prerequisites for the developmental task that concerns us here:
the formation of a self that can **receive** without disappearing.

### The central claim: receiving without witnessing trains danger

If early receiving happens without sufficient witnessing — whether due to absence, depression, rigid schedules,
dissociation, hostility, fear, or simply being overwhelmed — the infant learns a rule.
This rule is not a thought, but a bodily law:

> _When I open, I am alone._
> _When I need, there is no one to hold it._
> _When I am affected, I lose continuity._

In later life, such a rule can remain hidden because it is selectively triggered.
A person may function well in domains organised around action, achievement, performance, or control.
The rule becomes visible at the precise moment receptivity is required:
being comforted, being cared for, being the focus of attention, being touched, being “given to”,
and — interestingly — when being erotically aroused and then attempting to receive.

This clarifies why, in our case, “doing” could often proceed, while “receiving” repeatedly activated panic.

### Witnessing as a “third term” that prevents two failures

Developmental and relational literature repeatedly circles a structural point that can be stated plainly:
if witnessing is absent, the system tends to fall into one of two failures.

1. **Abandonment**
   Receiving occurs, but without support. The infant’s arousal and need have nowhere to go.
   Later, openness is avoided because it predicts being alone with intensity.

2. **Invasion / capture**
   Attention is present, but it is not safe. The infant is attended to as an extension, an object, or a problem.
   Later, receiving becomes dangerous because it predicts loss of boundaries, even annihilation as a separate self.

In practice, many histories involve mixtures of both.
The crucial point is that **witnessing is what prevents the dyad collapsing into either abandonment or capture.**
Witnessing functions as a _third term._
Not as a third person necessarily, but a third function:
the capacity for attuned presence that neither withdraws nor invades.

This “thirdness” will later become relevant in mythic terms,
but here it is enough to note that the nervous system appears to require it as a condition for safe receptivity.

### Why we name this “mother”, even when the issue is not “mother”

Calling this “the witnessing mother” may appear culturally and emotionally loaded.
The term can be misunderstood as an accusation or as a narrow claim about mothers.
That is not the intention. We use the word “mother” for two reasons:

1. **Developmental realism**
   In most lifelines, the mother (or primary caregiver in a maternal role) is
   the earliest and most influential context for receiving: feeding, holding, and soothing.

2. **Archetypal realism**
   The “mother” is also a symbolic position that cultures organise:
   how receptivity, dependency, and vulnerability are regulated or denied and thereby shamed.
   (Soon we will argue that cultural narratives about mothering, sacrifice, and gender
   shape what witnessing becomes possible.)

Thus, “mother” here names both a developmental role and a cultural archetype.
As we have already briefly discussed,
the role in question is not reducible to a biological mother but to the primary attachment figure,
whose sensitive responsiveness organises the infant’s regulatory world.[^Ainsworth]
Meta-analytic work has repeatedly shown that caregiver sensitivity and
reflective capacity predict attachment security,
while frightening, inconsistent, or withdrawn caregiving
increases the likelihood of insecurity and disorganisation.[^vanIJzendoorn] [^MainSolomon]
What is transmitted across generations is therefore not
a "mythic curse" but patterned relational expectations,
carried in internal working models and partially mediated by caregiving behaviour
and reflected as archetypical images in myths and the Jungian subcounsciousness.[^Assmann] [^Tillich]

In this light, we use the term _mother wound_ as a synthetic shorthand,
partly aligning with the pop healing scene and literature.
Hence, herein the term designates a _configuration in which early receptivity was insufficiently witnessed_.
As we briefly discussed above, while clearly multicausal, such conditions are well described in attachment and trauma literature and
are associated with later difficulties in affect regulation, dependency, and embodied intimacy.[^LyonsRuth] [^Norman]

Intergenerational research further suggests that these patterns persist
not primarily through conscious ideology but through implicit relational procedures:
caregivers tend to reproduce, in modified form, the attachment representations they themselves carry.[^Verhage-et-al]
When reflective functions and social support are limited,
the capacity to witness receptivity may narrow across generations,
even in the absence of overt abuse.
What is handed down is a diminished tolerance for another’s openness, and, with it, for one’s own.

Hence, within the argument of this essay,
the _mother wound_ names the intergenerational transmission of difficulties in witnessing receptivity.
It refers to a relational deficit where openness fails to meet stable recognition, containment, and non-violation.
The term is therefore both developmental and archetypal:
developmental in mechanism, archetypal in the forms through which cultures symbolise, deny, or moralise dependency
Importantly, we surmise that this deficit is often subtle and culturally normalised, as we argue in more detail below.

### Relation to fragmentation

The relevance of witnessing becomes sharper when we recall the fragmentation of the mind described in our case.
When early experience cannot be integrated the system does not simply remember.
The system segregates, splits into disconnected pieces, some of which seemingly disappear.
Later, when such a shutdown relaxes, what has been segregated can become visible,
first through dissociation, parts, and discontinuities of memory.

From this perspective, fragmentation is not only a symptom.
It is also evidence of how early receiving was handled:
by splitting experience into compartments that could survive only separately,
as they were not being held together by a reliable other.

This does not explain everything about DID, nor does it reduce it to a single cause of DID.
It does, however, align with our central theme:
the integrity of selfhood depends on the early conditions, under which receptivity was or was not possible.

### A witnessing summary

At this point we can summarise the situation in one sentence:

> The body needs a witness before it allows deep receiving.

Psychology can describe the mechanism. Polyvagal theory can describe the state transitions.
Part of the phenomenon can be iconified with the term _mother wound_, as has largely happened in the pop healing scene.

What remains is to articulate how cultures symbolise — or rather fail to symbolise — this need.
That is where myth becomes relevant: myths are not evidence for individual causation,
but they are repositories of cultural solutions and cultural blind spots.

In the next section, we examine a set of mythic structures
in which witnessing is explicit, absent, or contested, and ask what they reveal.
We focus on receptivity, fragmentation, and the possibility of receiving without loss of self.

## Mythic Supporting Structures

The psychological account above describes _mechanisms_
(shutdown, alarm, containment failure, fragmentation).
Myths do something different:
they encode _cultural solutions and blind spots_ in narrative forms.[^Geertz]
Here we use myths neither as proofs nor as diagnostic templates,
but as **maps of relational and existential structure**.

Our working hypothesis is simple:
where a culture has stable symbols for **witnessed receptivity**,
the transition into receiving is more likely to be narratable and therefore regulatable.
Where such symbols are weak or distorted,
the same human dynamics may become lived as private pathology.

Furthermore, we propose that this relation is not merely unidirectional.
Myths and cultural symbols do not merely _mirror_ psychological structures; they help stabilise them.[^Geertz] [^Bruner]
Where a culture lacks durable symbols for witnessed receptivity,
developmental compromises may crystallise more easily into trauma-patterns.
Once formed, those trauma-patterns in turn reinforce the very values, like control, efficiency, and domination,
that the culture continues to symbolise.

In this way, individual defensive neural organisations and
collective symbolic orders become coupled in a feedback loop:
cultural narratives shape what modes of being are considered legitimate or dangerous,
while traumatised nervous systems silently reproduce and normalise those same narratives.
The myths, introduced below, shall therefore be read not as ancient stories,
but as structural diagrams of this reciprocal formation.

Hence, the following myths are introduced as **operators** —
each highlights one structural element relevant to our case.
By ‘operator’ we herein mean a _recurrent structural move_ within a narrative
that reorganises relational possibility:
a function that alters how receiving, agency, and witnessing become possible.
Hence, they may be considered as _transformation rules_ in the structural sense described by Lévi-Strauss:
narrative functions that reorganise relational oppositions without eliminating them.[^LeviStrauss]

We first consider how Nishubur witnesses Ianna and provides a rescue coventant,
continuing to the meaning of Avalokiteśvara, Tārā, and Lemminkäinen's mother.
Finally, we consider Isis and Osiris and especially the role of Seth.
(See the appendices for brief narratives of the myths covered.)

[^Bruner: Bruner, Jerome. _Acts of Meaning_. Harvard University Press, 1990.]

### Inanna’s descent and Ninshubur: the protocol of witnessed descent

**Operator:** _the covenant of a rescue protocol_ — descent is permitted because return is guaranteed.

Among the myths surveyed here,
Inanna’s descent is the clearest narrative encoding of a principle that our case makes painfully concrete:
**before one enters a state of deep receptivity, one installs the witness.**
The myth does not treat descent as a private act of courage or surrender.
It treats descent as a regulated venture with explicit safeguards.

#### The story in structural terms

In the common outline of the myth (across variants),
Inanna chooses to descend to the underworld, the realm of Ereshkigal.
Before she goes, she does something that is easy to miss if one reads the story as mere drama:

- She appoints a witness and ally, **Ninshubur**, and
- she gives a **clear protocol**: if I do not return after a set time, you must raise alarm and seek help.

In other words, she does not go “naked into dissolution” without a tether.
She secures **a third term**, outside the underworld, who will act on her behalf.

Only then does she descend.
At the gates she is progressively stripped of her regalia:
crown, jewellery, symbols of power, and identity.
The underworld is the place where ordinary sovereignty does not function.
Eventually she is killed and hung (in some tellings, displayed) — a total collapse of agency and status.

If we read this psychologically,
the myth is not subtle: the underworld is a zone where control fails.
If we read it somatically, it is the zone where the system cannot maintain familiar organisation.
In both readings, descent is not “a deepening” but a **threshold crossing**
into a region in which the self cannot rely on its usual strategies.

This is precisely why the witness matters.

#### “Install the witness first” as an explicit rule

In the essay’s terms,
the ventral threshold is dangerous when receiving is attempted without sufficient witnessing.
In Inanna’s descent, the myth insists on the opposite ordering:

1) covenant and witness,
2) then surrender of power,
3) then the encounter with annihilation,
4) then retrieval.

This ordering is the essential operator of the myth:
a structural rule that reorganises what becomes possible for the self at the threshold of surrender
The myth thus encodes not a moral, but a sequence, a structure: covenant before surrender.

In our case, the nervous system behaves as if it has learned: “when I open, I am alone.”

Inanna behaves as if she knows: “I will not go alone. If I cannot return by my own power, someone will act for me.”

This should not be taken to suggest that Mesopotamian society as a whole provided such witnessing in practice. The myth preserves a structural insight, not a sociological description. The very need to encode rescue protocols may indicate that such safety was fragile or rare.

This is not merely “support”. It is a _structural guarantee of reversibility_.

#### Reversibility: why the three-day boundary matters

In several tellings, a temporal boundary is explicit (often three days and three nights).
Whatever the exact number, the point is not chronology but containment:
**a limit is placed on un-witnessed exposure**.

This resonates with the bodily “sampling delay” described in our case above:
tens of seconds in which the system tests whether safety will be present.
In the myth, the “test period” is longer and symbolic, but the logic is comparable:
there is a window within which return must be secured,
or the system will treat the descent as lethal.

A key implication follows:

> Deep receiving is not only about surrender; it is about surrender _with a retrieval path_.

This is why the myth belongs in this essay.
It encodes, in narrative form, what trauma work often rediscovers clinically:
openness must be **bounded** and **recoverable**.

#### Ninshubur as the witness-function and not merely just as a helper

Ninshubur is not important because she is loyal.
She is important because she represents a function:

- She remains outside the underworld (outside collapse).
- She keeps time (tracks continuity when the self cannot).
- She holds the intention and the story when Inanna cannot hold it.
- She mobilises wider resources when the dyad is insufficient.

Psychologically, Ninshubur looks like what Winnicott and Bion describe in technical terms:
a holding and containing other.
Somatically, she is the “external ventral anchor” that prevents a descent from becoming total shutdown.
Archetypally, she is the witness who neither invades nor abandons.

Crucially, Ninshubur is not Inanna’s “strength”.
She is an _other_.
This matters for our theme: the body does not stabilise on the receiving threshold through willpower.
It stabilises when an other remains reliably present.

#### Retrieval through mediation (Enki and the emissaries)

In many versions, Ninshubur does not storm the underworld herself.
She goes to higher powers (often Enki), who create small,
subtle emissaries that can enter the underworld without triggering its defences.
These emissaries do something psychologically sophisticated:
they do not argue with Ereshkigal, nor do they dominate her.
They **attune**; they mirror her cries; they “sit with” her pain until the release becomes possible.

Whatever one makes of the details,
the operator is consistent with the essay’s central claim:
regulation near annihilation does not occur through force.
It occurs through **non-invading witnessing that stays present with affect**.

This is the opposite of Seth’s method of efficiency and domination; see below.
It is also distinct from mere comfort.
It is a disciplined form of presence that allows the underworld to be contacted without being conquered.

#### The cost of return: substitution and the problem of contracts

In the myth, return is not free. A substitution is demanded.
This is an uncomfortable feature and relevant to our essay’s cultural layer:
systems of descent and return often involve contracts, debts, and sacrificial economies.

We will not over-interpret this here, but two observations are useful:

- The myth acknowledges that deep receiving and deep transformation occur
  within a larger order that demands accounting.
- Return without changing the order can simply relocate the cost elsewhere.

This links forward to our later cultural argument:
if a culture lacks stable symbols for witnessed receptivity,
it may default to Seth-like economies, where someone always pays,
where someone is always sacrificed and must be dominated.
The Inanna myth indicates that developing practices where receiving can be safe,
without creating a new victim, is indeed possible and even desirable.

#### Why this myth matters for “witnessed receptivity”

Inanna’s descent is not primarily a myth about feminine power, sexuality, or heroism (though it touches all of these).
For this essay, it is a myth about **the prerequisites for surrender**:

- surrender without witnessing becomes annihilation,
- surrender with a witness becomes descent-with-return,
- and the witness is not a sentimental addition but the enabling structure.

In clinical terms, this myth provides an image of what the body demands at the ventral threshold:
not a command to relax, but a covenant that makes receiving survivable.

### Avalokiteśvara: witnessing without capture

**Operator:** *the hearing field* — presence that receives suffering without appropriating it.

If Inanna’s descent encodes the **precondition** for surrender (“install the witness first”),
Avalokiteśvara encodes the **quality** of that witness.
Here the question is no longer _whether_ witnessing is present,
but _how_ witnessing can occur without becoming invasion, control, or erasure.

Avalokiteśvara (literally “the one who looks/hears downward”)
is defined not by action, conquest, or purity,
but by an unusual posture:
remaining present to suffering without turning away
and without converting that suffering into a project of domination.

This posture is structurally distinct from both abandonment and capture,
the two failures we identified earlier.

#### Hearing rather than fixing

In most tellings, Avalokiteśvara is characterised by *hearing the cries of the world*.
The emphasis is not on eliminating suffering through force,
nor on transcending it by withdrawal,
but on staying relationally available to it.

This is critical for our argument.
What is modelled here is not intervention first, but **reception**:
suffering is allowed to arrive, to be registered,
without being immediately acted upon or reorganised.

In psychological terms, this corresponds closely to the witnessing function we described earlier:

- presence without intrusion,
- attention without demand,
- responsiveness without capture.

Avalokiteśvara does not require the witnessing other
to perform, surrender, improve, or even change.
The witnessing itself is already the primary act.

#### Why this matters for receiving

From the body’s point of view,
receiving becomes dangerous when attention predicts either abandonment
(“nothing will hold this”) or invasion (“this will take over me”).
Avalokiteśvara offers a third configuration: attention that does not seize.

This is subtle but essential.
Many traumatised systems experience _attention itself_ as a threat,
because attention historically meant scrutiny, demand, or extraction.
In such systems, even care can feel dangerous
if it carries an implicit expectation of gratitude, compliance, or change.
Culturally, please note that Avalokiteśvara's presence is something
structurally distinct from the dominant Western norms;
see Section "Sovereign Gaze", further below.

Avalokiteśvara models a witnessing that makes no demand.
The cries are heard because they exist,
not because they must be resolved in order to justify the witness.

In the language of this essay,
this provides a symbolic grammar for **receiving while remaining a subject**.

#### Fragmentation and the refusal of collapse

In some Mahāyāna narratives,
Avalokiteśvara is said to shatter upon encountering the immensity of suffering,
only to be reconstituted into many forms.
Regardless of how literally one takes this,
the motif is strikingly relevant:
witnessing suffering without adequate structure risks fragmentation.

The myth does not deny this risk.
Instead, it responds by multiplying the capacity to witness
rather than withdrawing from the task.

This resonates with our case history:
when defensive shutdown relaxed,
fragmentation became temporarily more visible.
The solution was not to retreat back into numbness,
nor to force integration prematurely,
but to develop forms of presence that could _hold more without collapsing._

Avalokiteśvara symbolises precisely this:
the expansion of witnessing capacity
so that reception does not overwhelm the system.

#### Witnessing as non-dual, but not annihilating

Avalokiteśvara is often associated with non-dual insight,
yet this non-duality does not erase relational asymmetry.
There is still one who hears and one who cries.
What dissolves is not difference,
but the compulsion to dominate or withdraw from it.

This distinction is important.
We are not arguing for a mystical dissolution of subjectivity as the solution to trauma.
On the contrary, premature dissolution can reactivate panic and fragmentation,
as the case we study shows.
Such dissolution may later unfold,
but it cannot substitute for trauma integration.

Avalokiteśvara offers a possibility:
a form of awareness that is spacious enough to receive suffering
_without requiring the receiver to disappear._

#### Cultural significance

Within the wider cultural field,
Avalokiteśvara provides a rare counter-symbol to the logic of efficiency and control.
Here, care is not justified by outcomes, and witnessing is not contingent on productivity.

In cultural environments structured around performance, conquest, or self-mastery,
this figure may be quietly subversive or it may functions as a counter-symbol.
Its existence does not necessarily demonstrate that such environments were free of domination;
rather, it often reveals an ongoing tension between witnessing and control within the tradition itself.

This cultural absence of Avalokiteśvara like witnessing helps explain why, in our case,
the emergence of receptive openness is experienced existentially threatening:
the body was moving toward a configuration
for which its surrounding culture offers little stable symbolic support.

### Tārā: sovereign receptivity (agency without conquest)

**Operator:** _the third position_ — agency and receptivity without role captivity.

If Avalokiteśvara clarifies the _quality_ of witnessing (presence without capture),
**Tārā** clarifies something even more destabilising for inherited cultural logic:
**receiving and agency need not be split.**

Where much of patriarchal symbolism forces a choice —
either active and sovereign _or_ receptive and vulnerable —
Tārā embodies a configuration in which responsiveness, care, and protection
do not require surrendering subjecthood.

This makes her uniquely relevant to the phenomenon we are tracing.

#### Emergence from compassion, not hierarchy

In many tellings, Tārā arises from Avalokiteśvara’s tears,
shed upon witnessing the suffering of beings.
Symbolically, she is not created through command, lineage, or conquest,
but through _responsive compassion_ encountering the limits of endurance.

This origin matters.
Tārā does not stand “above” suffering, nor does she merely witness it.
She emerges _from_ the very act of witnessing,
as a form capable of responding without reverting to domination.

In the language of this essay,
Tārā represents what happens when witnessing does not collapse into passivity,
and agency does not harden into control.

#### Action without domination

Unlike many sovereign or crisis-driven figures in mythic traditions, Tārā acts quickly.
She protects, intervenes, rescues.
But her action is not organised around hierarchy, punishment, or extraction.
It is **relational action**:
movement that preserves the subjecthood of both self and other.

This distinction is subtle but decisive.

Tārā demonstrates that responsiveness need not collapse into passivity,
and agency need not harden into control.
Her speed does not arise from panic.
Her force does not arise from rivalry.
Her intervention does not require the humiliation or subordination of what she protects.

In structural terms,
Tārā shows that effective action does not have to be purchased
at the cost of receptivity.

This turns out to be very important for our later political argument.
For now, it is enough to observe that
the split between doing and receiving is not a universal necessity,
but a particular configuration that myths sometimes reproduce
and sometimes transcend.

#### Why Tārā matters for receiving

For traumatised systems,
receiving often predicts loss of agency.
To open is to risk being overrun, used, or erased.
As a result, receiving is postponed until total safety is guaranteed —
which, in embodied life, rarely occurs.

Tārā provides a symbolic resolution to this impasse.
She embodies **receiving without collapse**:
one can remain responsive, open, and relational
while still retaining the capacity to act, refuse, and protect.

This is particularly important for our case.
The person’s panic did not arise because they feared intimacy per se,
but because receiving seemed to require *disappearing*.
Tārā symbolises a third option:
receiving that does not entail surrendering sovereignty.

#### Beyond gendered roles

Although Tārā is explicitly feminine in iconography,
her relevance here is not reducible to gender.
She does not simply invert a masculine hierarchy
by installing a feminine ruler in its place.
Instead, she dissolves the _structure_ that required the split in the first place.

From this perspective,
the non-binary emergence in our case can be read not as an identity preference,
but as a **structural response**:
when inherited gender roles cannot hold receptive subjecthood,
the system seeks configurations that are not bound by them.

Tārā offers a mythic precedent for such a move.
She legitimises a form of existence
that is neither penetrator nor penetrated,
neither controller nor object,
but responsive, present, and capable of action.

#### Tārā and the risk of sentimentalisation

It is important to avoid sentimentalising Tārā
as merely nurturing or gentle.
Many forms of Tārā are fierce.
They act decisively, sometimes violently,
but always in defence of life rather than the preservation of order.

This matters for our argument.
Witnessed receptivity is not equivalent to softness.
It is compatible with boundaries, refusal, and protection.
What distinguishes it is not the absence of force,
but the refusal to sacrifice subjecthood in order to deploy it.

#### Cultural significance

In cultures dominated by binaries —
doer/receiver, subject/object, active/passive —
Tārā remains difficult to integrate.
She destabilises the assumption that agency must be purchased
at the cost of receptivity.

Tārā does not solve the problem of receiving.
She names the possibility that the problem points toward:
**a form of subjecthood in which receiving, acting, and witnessing are no longer mutually exclusive.**

### Lemminkäinen’s mother: re-membering without domination

**Operator:** *re-membering* — the retrieval and reassembly of what has been scattered when agency has already failed.

When the preceding myths focus on the **conditions that make descent survivable** (Inanna),
the **quality of witnessing** (Avalokiteśvara),
and **agency that does not cancel receptivity** (Tārā),
the Lemminkäinen cycle addresses a different moment:
**what happens after the collapse has already occurred?**

Here the question is no longer how to descend safely,
but how life can be restored once fragmentation is complete.

#### The SKVR pattern

A bit unlike in Lönnroth's heavily edited Kalevala,
in the original Finnish tradition (recorded in the Suomen Kansan Vanhat Runot (SKVR)),
Lemminkäinen is not primarily a moral hero or a disciplined culture-bearer.
He is impulsive, erotic, boastful, and reckless — a figure who repeatedly exceeds limits.

His death is not a tragic accident in the modern sense.
It is the **predictable outcome of uncontained vitality**.
He enters the realm of Tuonela (land of death) without adequate protection, is struck down,
and his body is cut to pieces and scattered into the river of death.

This is literal disintegration.  Dying without any return, ever.

#### The mother’s action: gathering, not judging

What follows is the decisive move of the myth.
No father avenges.
No god intervenes.
No heroic resurrection occurs through strength or cleverness.

Instead, **the mother goes to the river**.

She does not debate whether the son deserved his fate.
She does not demand repentance.
She does not attempt to reverse death through power.
She does not moralise.

She **gathers**.

Piece by piece, she searches the river, finds the fragments, and reassembles the body.
Only after the body is made whole again does life return.

In SKVR variants, this labour is slow, repetitive, and bodily.
The emphasis is not on triumph,
but on **persistence in relation to what has fallen apart**.

#### The absence of the seer

This myth encodes something distinct from the earlier ones:

- Inanna shows how to _avoid irreversible collapse._
- Avalokiteśvara shows how to _remain present to suffering._
- Tārā shows how to _act without domination._

Lemminkäinen’s mother shows what must happen **when none of that was available in time**.

The system has already fragmented. Agency is gone.
The self is not merely threatened; it is literally in pieces at the bottom of the river.

At this point, witnessing alone is no longer sufficient.
What is required is **re-membering**: the active, patient restoration of continuity.

This is crucial for understanding later-life trauma work, like in our case.
Many individuals do not arrive at therapy at the threshold of descent.
They arrive _after_ fragmentation has already occurred.
The task is no longer to install a witness before surrender,
but to retrieve what was split off so survival could continue.

#### Re-membering without conquest

The mother’s labour is striking for what it does _not_ include:

- no coercion,
- no purification,
- no conquest of the underworld,
- no attempt to dominate death.

She does not even try to defeat Tuonela.  She works **with what remains**.

This distinguishes re-membering from heroic rescue or planned surrender.
It is not about restoring sovereignty.
It is about restoring **continuity of being**.

This directly parallels the clinical process
in which dissociated parts are not eliminated,
overruled, or forced into coherence,
but slowly gathered into a shared field of awareness.

#### The limit of the myth (and why it matters)

The myth leaves an important question unresolved.

The one who performs the re-membering is the mother.
The one who is restored is the son.

The myth does not imagine the receiver-self re-membering itself,
nor does it imagine a non-maternal relational field
capable of performing this function.

In other words, the myth solves fragmentation,
but **only through a maternal archetype**.

In our case, this is very much both a gift and a limitation.

It gives us a powerful image of what re-membering requires:
unconditional persistence,
non-moralising presence,
and a refusal to abandon what has fallen apart.

But it does not yet answer the question that emerges in our case study:

> What happens when the receiver is no longer a “son,”
> when the maternal position is unavailable,
> and when identity itself has become unstable?

(Footnote: This unresolved tension prepares the ground
for the next mythic movement — Isis and Osiris —
where re-membering occurs under explicitly patriarchal conditions,
and where restoration is possible
only by breaking the old sexual-political order.)

Lemminkäinen’s mother stands as a **transitional figure**:
she shows that re-membering is possible,
but also reveals how dependent that possibility still is
on a maternal archetype that later cultures have struggled to sustain.

### Isis and Osiris: reassembly under a hostile order

**Operator:** _repair without restoration_ — re-membering that cannot return to the prior sexual-political order.

If Lemminkäinen’s mother shows re-membering in an at most weakly patriarchal setting,
the myth of Isis and Osiris takes place explicitly _inside_ the patriarchal regime.
Here, fragmentation is not accidental or impulsive.
It is **inflicted** — politically, sexually, and cosmologically —
by Seth as the agent of domination, rivalry, and crisis-logic.

This difference matters.
The myth is not only about death and resurrection,
but about what kinds of repair are possible
once an order organised around power, lineage, and control has already taken hold.

#### Osiris: a receptive king who cannot survive the regime

In many tellings, Osiris is a benevolent and civilising ruler.
He teaches agriculture, law, and ritual;
he brings order through _continuity_ rather than threat and terror.
Crucially for our purposes, Osiris is not a conquering hero.
He is a **receptive** figure: associated with fertility, cyclical renewal, and stability.

This makes him intolerable to Seth.
Seth does not merely envy Osiris;
he represents a different organising principle altogether:
force, separation, crisis-efficiency, and zero-sum power.

Osiris is not killed in battle.
He is **trapped, sealed, dismembered, and scattered**.
The violence is surgical and symbolic:
the body is cut into pieces and distributed across the land,
making simple restoration impossible.

This is not collapse through recklessness (as with Lemminkäinen).
It is **fragmentation as structural strategy**.

#### Isis: gathering without illusion

Isis’ response echoes the labour of Lemminkäinen’s mother,
but under radically different conditions.

She searches.
She gathers the scattered pieces.
She refuses erasure.

However, unlike Lemminkäinen's mother,
Isis does not recover _everything_.
The phallus is missing — eaten, lost, dissolved.
She fashions a substitute,
sufficient not for restoration of the old order,
but for conception.

(Footnote: The Osiris cycle exists in multiple historical strata,
from Old Kingdom Pyramid Texts (3rd millennium BCE)
through Coffin Texts and New Kingdom temple theology to later Greco-Roman retellings.
Egyptological scholarship generally agrees that the myth was never fixed in a single canonical form,
but evolved in liturgical, regional, and political contexts.
The explicit narrative of the phallus being eaten by fish is
most fully attested in Plutarch’s *De Iside et Osiride* (1st–2nd century CE),
though the broader motif of incomplete bodily restoration is already implicit in earlier sources.
See, e.g., J. Gwyn Griffiths (ed. and trans.), *Plutarch’s De Iside et Osiride*
(University of Wales Press, 1970), and Jan Assmann, *Death and Salvation in Ancient Egypt* (Cornell University Press, 2005),
for discussion of textual layering and theological development.

For the purposes of the present argument,
the decisive element is not anatomical detail but structural irreversibility:
Osiris is reassembled yet cannot return to embodied kingship.
The impossibility of full restoration within a lineage-centred sexual-political order
is mythically encoded as bodily incompleteness.
The later emphasis on the missing phallus may reflect an increasing explicitness in
how kingship, virility, and legitimacy were symbolically fused,
rather than the invention of the structural motif itself.)

This detail, incomplete restoration, is often sanitised or rushed past.
In our reading, it is central.

The myth states plainly:
**the previous sexual order cannot be reassembled.**

Re-membering is possible.
Continuation is possible.
But _return_ is not.

#### The missing phallus as structural fact

The absent phallus is not merely anatomical.
It symbolises the impossibility of reinstating
the old proto-patriarchal sexual contract intact.

Osiris can become lord of the underworld.
Horus can be born.
Lineage can continue.
But Osiris cannot return to embodied kingship as it was.

This directly parallels the dynamic in our case study:
receptivity can be recovered,
life can continue,
but the prior gendered and sexual identity
cannot simply be restored.

Repair does not mean regression.

#### Horus: continuity without revenge

Horus is often read as the avenger of Osiris.
For us, that reading is very incomplete.

Horus is primarily _the Eye_ —
a symbol of seeing, continuity, and order restored
without reinstating the old body of the father.
The various versions of the myth oscillates here:
sometimes Horus enacts revenge,
sometimes he represents a new equilibrium.

For our purposes,
Horus stands at a threshold:
either he becomes another Seth
(repeating domination),
or he becomes a different form of continuity
that carries forward without denying loss.

The myth does not resolve this ambiguity cleanly.
That unresolved tension is itself instructive.

#### Summary and moving forward

Isis and Osiris bring several threads together:

- Re-membering **is possible** even under hostile conditions.
- Witnessed labour can restore continuity but not identity per se.
- Patriarchal orders can be survived, but not simply repaired from within.
- Attempts to bypass receptivity (Seth’s logic) reproduce violence rather than life.

In contrast to Lemminkäinen’s mother,
Isis operates inside a regime where witnessing is contested,
sexuality is politicised,
and power seeks to reproduce itself by force.

This makes her labour both more constrained and more revealing.

Together, the myths of Lemminkäinen and Isis show two limits:

- Re-membering requires a witnessing other.
- But when the cultural order itself is hostile to receptivity,
  re-membering alone is insufficient
  to restore a liveable form of embodied subjecthood.

## Seth’s Objection: the logic of domination against witnessed receptivity

In the preceding sections,
we have traced what the body appears to require in order to allow deep receiving:
a witness, a third term, and a relational field that can hold openness without collapse.
At this point, a counter-logic must be made explicit.

We name this counter-logic **Seth’s objection**.

For us, Seth, the murderer, is not a mythological villain nor as a psychological pathology.
We treat him here as a **principle**: the logic of crisis and hierarchy that asserts itself
whenever relational processes are judged too slow, too costly, or too uncertain.

Seth’s objection can be summarised simply:

> *Witnessing is a luxury. In conditions of danger, power must act first. Receiving must be controlled or eliminated.*

This objection is not trivial.
In conditions of acute danger, hierarchy and decisive action save lives.
The problem begins when a logic designed for crisis becomes
the default organiser of human life — including intimacy.
As we have already seen in the figure of Tārā, mythic traditions do preserve another possibility:
swift agency that does not cancel receptivity.
Seth’s objection therefore marks not a _necessity_ of domination,
but a cultural, path dependent development narrative that has largely become prevasive.

### The core of the objection

Seth’s logic rests on a small number of claims,
which recur across myths, institutions, and embodied reactions:

- **Care is too slow.** In danger, hesitation kills. Regulation must be immediate.
- **Openness is weakness.** To receive without control is to expose oneself to harm.
- **Power must reproduce itself.** Continuity depends on enforcing lineage, order, and dominance.
- **The third term is inefficient.** Witnessing introduces delay, ambiguity, and negotiation.

Taken together, these claims form a coherent worldview.
In that worldview, receiving cannot be trusted.
It must either be tightly regulated or replaced by action.

### Mythic illustration: Seth’s counter-move

In the Osiris myth, Seth’s violence does not end with dismemberment.
After Isis has gathered the pieces of Osiris and secured continuity through conception,
Seth attempts different strategis of reproduction,
including one that bypasses relation, consent, and witnessing.

The so-called “lettuce incident” (see the Appendices) is often treated as comic or obscene.
Structurally, it is neither.

It is Seth’s answer to Isis’ labour:
**reproduction without witnessing**,
power without receptivity,
continuity enforced rather than held.
Symbolically, it portrays the use of sexuality as a weapon of domination.
It stages an attempt to bypass relation through coercive incorporation.
Here the myth exposes that strategy as hollow and self-defeating.

(Footnote: It can be argued that Seth's strategy became later more accepted and even legitimised.
See Section XXX, below.)

For us, this represents the clearest mythic image of what happens
when power tries to bypass the conditions of safe receiving.

This is a direct mythic analogue
to the panic that arises when receiving is demanded without witnessing:
the system reacts not with integration, but with alarm.
Seth attempts to assert dominance and continuity through unilateral impregnation,
without relational containment:
His placing of his semen into lettuce is a symbolic attempt to rape Horus.
It is wasted reproduction without witnessing, power without receptivity.
It is an act of using sexual power for mere dominance.

The result is not restored order, but exposure and judgement.
Seth’s attempt fails not because it lacks force, but because it lacks relation.
Mythically, this illustrates a recurring pattern:
when power tries to bypass the conditions of safe receiving,
it eventually produces only instability rather than continuity.

This is not a moral lesson. It is a structural one.
We are collectively witnessing this, right now.

### Why Seth appears at the threshold of receiving

Seth’s logic becomes most active precisely at the point
where control loosens and dependence becomes real.
This is true in myth, culture, and the body.

In the case under discussion, panic does not arise at “sex” as such.
It arises at the moment when receiving becomes possible:
when numbness lifts, when attention deepens, when agency risks softening.
At that point, Seth’s logic is activated internally.
The system moves to reassert control or to shut down entirely.
Culturally, similar logics have often attached stigma to receptive male sexuality,
precisely where penetration is equated with loss of sovereignty.
Historically, patriarchal sexual codes have frequently equated
receptivity in a male body with subordination.
In such frameworks, panic at the threshold of receiving as such is
easily moralised and condemned rather than understood.

It is the internalisation of Seth’s objection:
_this is too dangerous; restore dominance or withdraw._

### Seth’s two offers

When Seth’s logic takes over, it typically offers one of two solutions,
both of which preserve survival at the cost of receptivity:

1. **Armour (mobilisation)**
   Control, performance, domination, doing rather than receiving.

2. **Stone (shutdown)**
   Numbness, disappearance, endurance, avoidance of the threshold altogether.

Both are adaptive. Both are experienced internally as necessity rather than choice.
Neither allows receiving to remain safe.

### Cultural crystallisation: the patriarchal contract

At the cultural level, Seth’s objection solidifies into a familiar sexual-political contract:

- doer / receiver
- penetrator / penetrated
- subject / object

This contract is efficient. It reduces ambiguity.
It minimises the need for witnessing by predetermining roles.
It ensures continuity by subordinating receptivity to power.

For long historical periods, this logic has been stabilising.
It has also carried a cost: **receiving has become structurally perilous**,
especially when the receiver insists on remaining a subject.

In such a system, witnessed receptivity is not merely unsupported; it is actively destabilising.

### The boundary condition: where Seth is “right”

It is important to state this clearly: Seth’s logic has a legitimate domain.

In acute physical danger, warfare, catastrophe, and moments requiring immediate action,
witnessing often cannot be the governing principle.
Even relational responsiveness (cf. Tārā) has limits under acute threat.
Speed, hierarchy, and decisive action save lives, sometimes.
(Footnote: Ontological note: From certain consciousness-first perspectives (e.g. Kastrup; Faggin),
biological survival is not the ultimate good but an expression of compassion within manifestation.
However, that broader question exceeds well beyond the present argument,
which remains concerned with developmental and cultural structure rather than metaphysical hierarchy.)

The claim of this essay is not that Seth must disappear.
The central claim is much narrower and more precise:

> **Seth must not govern intimacy.**

When the logic of crisis dominates eros, care, and receiving, the body revolts.
Panic, shutdown, and fragmentation are not failures of will or maturity.
They are signals that the wrong organising principle is in charge.

Seth’s objection clarifies why witnessed receptivity is culturally difficult.
It competes directly with efficiency, dominance, and role clarity.
Where cultures lack stable symbols for receiving without self-loss,

Seth’s solutions — armour and stone — tend to prevail by default.
Today, they expand far beyound their useful scope.
When crisis-logic becomes ambient rather than exceptional,
systems lose the capacity to metabolise vulnerability without force.
Neither armour nor stone are permissive, which eventually leads to polarisation.

## The Sovereign Gaze

If Seth names the logic of domination in its mythic form,
the figure of the _sovereign_ names its historical crystallisation.
Across many mythic, royal, and theological traditions,
to be _seen_ by the king is both an elevation and a danger.
Recognition by a sovereign does not merely acknowledge; it incorporates.

In courtly and monarchical settings, attention flows vertically.
To be summoned, rewarded, or publicly recognised is simultaneously to be bound to hierarchy.
The sovereign gaze does not only illuminate.  It positions.

This structural asymmetry is not incidental.
It links visibility to obligation, recognition to debt, and favour to incorporation into power.

### The gift that indebts

Marcel Mauss’ classic study *The Gift* demonstrated that in archaic societies,
gifts are rarely free.[^Mauss]
To give is to assert relation.
To receive is to accept that relation.
To refuse is to insult.
To accept is to enter obligation.

In many gift economies, the most powerful figure gives the most lavish gift —
and thereby secures the deepest bond of indebtedness.
The receiver becomes structurally subordinate, not because of violence,
but because reciprocity under asymmetry cannot be equal.

This anthropological pattern clarifies something about the trauma around receiving.
If receiving historically implied debt, and debt implied subordination,
then the body’s hesitation before openness is not irrational.
It carries the echo of a long cultural grammar: to receive from above is to owe.

[^Mauss: Mauss, Marcel. _The Gift: Forms and Functions of Exchange in Archaic Societies_. 1925. English trans. W. D. Halls. Routledge, 1990.]

### Attention as incorporation

Under sovereign logic, attention is rarely neutral.
To be noticed is to be evaluated.
To be praised is to be claimed.
To be gifted is to be incorporated.

This differs fundamentally from the witnessing described earlier.
Witnessing preserves subjecthood.
Sovereign attention reorganises it.

When early care occurs under conditions
where attention predicts demand, evaluation, or role assignment,
the infant may learn that being seen is equivalent to being managed.
Receiving then becomes structurally ambiguous: is this nourishment, or is this capture?

### From myth to institution

The sovereign gaze does not disappear with the end of monarchies.
It persists in institutional forms:
merit systems, public recognition, performance hierarchies, reward structures,
and charismatic authority.[^Bourdieu]

Where recognition is tied to productivity, and care is granted conditionally,
receiving remains inseparable from evaluation.
One does not simply receive; one qualifies.
Over time, these structures become embodied and perceived as natural parts of the world.[^Foucault] [^Graeber]

The logic aligns closely with Seth’s objection:
witnessing is inefficient,
hierarchy clarifies,
and debt stabilises.[^Mauss] [^Bourdieu] [^Graeber]

The question this raises for our essay is not political in the narrow sense,
but structural:

> What happens to bodies that require receiving without debt,
> in cultures where recognition is inseparable from incorporation?[^Honneth]

### A structural distinction

We can now sharpen a distinction that will matter later:

- **Sovereign gaze**
  - Vertical, evaluative, binding.
  - Recognition produces obligation.
  - Receiving implies loss of autonomy.

- **Witnessing gaze**
  - Horizontal, non-extractive, non-binding.
  - Recognition preserves separateness.
  - Receiving does not entail debt.

If a culture stabilises primarily the sovereign form of attention,
then receptivity remains politically and somatically unstable.
The body may respond not with gratitude, but with alarm.

Note that we do not claim that hierarchy is _always_ pathological,
nor that sovereign structures have not had adaptive value.
We just contend only that where receiving is structurally tied to debt,
safe receptivity becomes difficult to symbolise.

### When receiving becomes dangerous

Across these myths we can already see a shared pattern:

- **Re-membering** (Kalevala mother; Isis): restoration of continuity after fragmentation.
- **Protocol / Thirdness** (Ninshubur; Horus as the “Eye”): a witness-function that prevents abandonment or capture.
- **Sovereign receptivity** (Avalokiteśvara; Tārā): receiving that remains present and does not dissolve into erasure.
- **Absolute sovereign** (Seth, the king): hierarchy binds receiving to debt, symbolically destroying voluntary safe surrendering.

Mythologically, this can be summarised as follows:

> Receiving is survivable only when surrender and return themselves are witnessed.

In a disciplinary society, the sovereign gaze is no longer embodied in a king but diffused as an internalised norm.
The self learns to supervise itself continuously, to anticipate evaluation, to remain legible.
Receiving becomes dangerous precisely because it suspends this supervisory stance.
To receive deeply is to loosen vigilance, to risk opacity, to enter a state not organised by performance.

Where cultures provide stable symbols for this loosening —
covenant before descent (Inanna and Ninshubur), witnessing without capture (Avalokiteśvara),
agency that does not cancel receptivity (Tārā) — surrender can remain reversible.
But where such symbols are weak, marginal, or privatised, receiving lacks public legitimacy.
It appears as regression, inefficiency, or loss of sovereignty.

In that situation, one dominant cultural template for receiving becomes surrender
to evaluative authority — the symbolic king.
Protection is promised in exchange for compliance.
Recognition is inseparable from incorporation.
Receiving collapses into submission.

If surrender is culturally intelligible only in hierarchical form, crisis-logic begins to generalise.
What was once reserved for exceptional danger becomes ambient.
Efficiency displaces witnessing.
Thirdness — mediation, delay, relational negotiation — is treated as weakness.
The sovereign principle expands beyond its legitimate domain.

In its most radical forms, this expansion hardens into political structure.
When receptivity itself is framed as threat —
when openness is equated with weakness, ambiguity with disorder, vulnerability with contamination —
systems drift toward eliminating what cannot be controlled.
Dehumanisation becomes thinkable.
Evaluation becomes total.
Moral ambiguity collapses into absolutism.

Historically, we have seen how such dynamics can culminate:
where only compliance guarantees protection,
and non-compliance — by act, belief, or mere being — is redefined as existential danger.
In those regimes, annihilation is no longer metaphorical.
It is enacted.

This linkage cannot be fully demonstrated here; it would take far too much space.
Yet, the structural affinity is difficult to ignore:
when cultures eradicate the symbolic and relational supports for safe receptivity,
and when crisis-logic becomes the organising grammar of collective life,
the space for plural, vulnerable, reversible subjecthood narrows.
What begins as an internal prohibition against receiving can
(and has been under certain historical conditions)
crystallise into fascistic forms
that eliminate vulnerability rather than witness it.

## Receiving in the culture of Control

What we have seen above has prepared the ground for examining our contemporary Western culture,
without kings yet saturated with evaluation.  While there are wider considerations (as we just saw),
here we focus on how our culture organises and fails to organise receiving,
at the symbolic, archetypal, and psychological (trauma) levels.
Above we outlined the structural risks when a control culture takes its most condensed and radical forms.
We now examine the everyday cultural conditions in which such dynamics either intensify or soften.

We ask a broad question: to which extend do our modern Western cultures possess
adequate symbolic, relational, and institutional supports for witnessed receptivity,
and how much the new emerging forms of identity and embodiment
are attempts to solve a problem that the existing structures can no longer hold.

### Control as pervasive background assumption

Compared to many other cultures, our contemporary western culture may be characterised as a culture of control.
The strong and wide control structures have brought us a lot (mostly material) good;
they have made possible such marvels as global trade and unforeseen level of material affluence.
At the same time, it has framed — as cultures do — what it means to be a human and good citizen.
It has created a normative structure that defines not only the values everyone is supposed to share
but also a multitude of institutions that are tactly assumed to be real and perpetual.

The role of control as a default cultural virtue emphasises
self-regulation, self-management, self-optimisation.
Adult autonomy is framed as independence from need. Every man is the architect of his own fortune.
Loss of control is framed as failure, immaturity, or danger, deeply receiving as a state that inherently threatens control.

In our culture, control is not only a virtue but an ambient norm.
Any adult overtly losing control, even temporarily, is considered impolite at minimum and lunatic at the other end,
depending on the depth and length of losing control.

A culture of control is not only a set of values; it is produced and tactly enforced by institutional pressures.
In Foucault’s terms, modern power operates no more by sovereign command but through discipline and normalisation:
the man learns to supervise himself and to equate autonomy with self-management. [^Foucault]
Rosa’s analysis of social acceleration clarifies why this supervision becomes pervasive:
under conditions of dynamic stabilisation, speed and efficiency are moralised because falling behind is structurally punished. [^Rosa]

Illouz shows how these logics enter intimacy itself:
romance and sexuality tend to become organised through choice, performance,
and evaluative comparison, narrowing the symbolic space for being affected without role pressure. [^Illouz]
Luhmann’s account of functional differentiation adds a final twist:
modern society lacks a shared public code for “receiving while remaining a subject”,
so difficulties of receptivity are privatised—medicalised, psychologised, or pushed into self-help—rather than culturally held. [^Luhmann]
Under such conditions, the body’s refusal of preceived openness appears as pathologisable symptom
instead of a somatic boundary enforcement against incompatible demands.

[^Rosa: Rosa, Hartmut. _Social acceleration: A new theory of modernity._ Columbia University Press, 2013.]

[^Illouz: Illouz, Eva. _Cold intimacies: The making of emotional capitalism._ Polity, 2007.]

### The Culture of Control: Everyday Observations

If one wished to observe the culture of control in its natural habitat,
one would not need to attend a military parade.
One could simply wake up on a Tuesday.

The day begins not with sunrise but with an alarm —
a small sovereign device that informs us that our body’s rhythms are, while interesting, secondary.
We do not wake because we are rested. We wake because the schedule has spoken.

Before standing up, many of us consult a screen.
The screen presents a curated list of obligations: emails, tasks, meetings, reminders.
It is, in effect, a quiet roll call.
One confirms attendance not by speaking, but by replying, scheduling, and optimising.

Breakfast is often taken not because hunger slowly ripens, but because time allows for seven minutes.
The commute is calculated. The route is optimised. Even leisure is pre-booked, tracked, and evaluated.
If one goes for a walk, one may measure steps. If one meditates, one may track minutes.
If one sleeps, one may review the data the next morning to determine whether one has slept correctly.

Control is rarely experienced as oppression. It is experienced as responsibility.

We manage calendars, bodies, relationships, finances, reputations.
We respond to notifications promptly because delay feels vaguely immoral.
We apologise for taking time.
We describe ourselves as “behind” even when no one is chasing us directly.

It is difficult to identify where the self ends and the ambient norm begins.
We discipline ourselves in order to remain employable, attractive, efficient, sane.

Public loss of control is handled with discomfort. Tears in a workplace meeting require explanation.
Anger must be reframed as “passion.”

Even intimacy is not entirely exempt.
Romantic life is organised through profiles, comparison, selection criteria.
Attraction is filtered through self-presentation.
One speaks of “working on oneself,” “improving communication,” “being emotionally available” —
all admirable aims, yet phrased as projects requiring management.

None of this is sinister. It has produced extraordinary coordination, prosperity, and safety.
It allows millions of strangers to cooperate without chaos.

Yet it establishes a background assumption: adulthood is maintaining functional control.
To be a competent subject is to regulate oneself continuously.

Within such an atmosphere, experiences that require loosening control,
e.g. grief that does not follow a schedule or erotic receptivity that cannot be optimised,
appear suspicious.
They resist measurement. They cannot be easily entered into a calendar.

The culture does not forbid such states. It simply fails to provide them with a stable public grammar.

As a result, when the body hesitates before deep receiving, the hesitation does not appear culturally legible.
It appears inefficient, regressive, and especially embarrassing.

The sovereign has not disappeared. It has been miniaturised and internalised.
It now travels in our pockets and speaks in our own voice.

And so the day proceeds: controlled, mostly by ourselves, and subtly, almost politely, mandated from everywhere.

### Efficiency, speed, and the moralisation of action

Growth and efficiency are two pillars of our capitalist value system.
Modern economies rely on growth and efficiency for stability.

At the everyday level, the economic system and the cultural norms
force us to increase efficiency, to work harder and faster.
This applies more-or-less to everyone, from the unemployed who is supposed
submit hundreds of applications to receive benefits to the superrich
who work 16 hours a day, though supposedly because they just like it.

In our culture, speed and efficiency are treated as moral goods, not just practical ones.
Slowness, hesitation, and waiting are framed as wasteful or indulgent.
According to our norms, a slow person is a lazy one, making it morally acceptable for them to fall into poverty.
The norm narrates this as their deliberate choice, independent of the actual circumstances.

We have a strong cultural bias for preferring doing over being, acting over sensing, and producing over receiving.

Under cultural acceleration, time becomes a scarce moral resource;
experiences that require unhurried attunement—care,
erotic receiving, grief, integration—are pressured into efficiency formats,
and the nervous system responds with shutdown or panic when forced past its tolerances. [^Rosa]

This raises an implicit question: What happens to forms of experience whose regulation depends on slowness, repetition, and reversible exposure?

### The narrowing of acceptable dependency

Modern Western cultures have not eliminated dependency; they have tightly zoned it.

Dependency is widely tolerated and even expected in infancy.
It is temporarily legitimised in acute illness.
It is forgiven in moments of recognised catastrophe.
However, outside these clearly demarcated zones,
sustained dependency is considered culturally suspicious or at least ambiguous.

Adult needs are expected to be brief, goal-oriented, and controlled. [^Rosa]
An adult may require help, but one must also demonstrate progress.
Therapy is acceptable, but only if it leads to improvement.
Grief is legitimate, provided it does not last “too long.”
Financial support is defensible, provided it is framed as temporary.
Even friendships often operate under the tacit assumptions that reciprocity must remain roughly balanced and visibly so.

In this structure, dependency becomes easily medicalised, psychologised, or moralised. [^Foucault]
If one cannot function independently, the problem is diagnosed.
If one relies too heavily on another, the relation is perceived as co-dependency.
If one remains attached, the issue is reframed as immaturity. [^Honneth]

The ensuing difficulties for witnessed receptivity are subtle but consequential.
Deep receiving resembles dependency when perceived through prevailing cultural norms.
It involves softening, leaning, and allowing oneself to be affected.
Earlier we traced how, in development, repeated experiences of misattuned or conditional care
can lead the nervous system to equate leaning with risk.
When dependency is culturally framed as deficit or immaturity, that early learning finds confirmation.
The body may therefore register sustained openness not as connection but as potential regression,
and remaining in need not as relational depth but as exposure to judgement.

Deep receiving, therefore, has no stable cultural home.
It is either becomes compressed into exceptional states or redirected into self-sufficiency.
The result is that ordinary, mutual vulnerability becomes structurally under-supported.

[^Honneth: Honneth, Axel. _The struggle for recognition: The moral grammar of social conflicts._ MIT press, 1996.]

### Sexuality under performance norms

Intimacy, too, does not escape these pressures.
In contemporary Western contexts, sexuality is increasingly organised through
the logics of performance, optimisation, and evaluation.
Desire is discussed in terms of arousal levels. Encounters are measured through satisfaction.
Technique, responsiveness, and outcome become implicit benchmarks. [^Illouz]
Of course, none of this is inherently pathological. For example, clear communication and attentiveness can deepen connection.
The difficulty arises when _sexuality is subtly formatted as a site of achievement._

Under performance norms, erotic life risks becoming an arena in which one must succeed.
The emphasis falls on doing well—being attentive, confident, competent—rather than on being affected.
Even the language for surrendering into sensation, for being held without agenda,
for receiving without role pressure remains comparatively thin. [^Giddens]

Romantic capitalism intensifies this formatting.
Intimate partners are selected through profiles, compared across criteria, and evaluated according to perceived value.
The self becomes a project of presentation and continuous refinement.
Within such an economy, receptive dependency can resemble loss of market-agency.
To be overtaken by desire, to need without calculation,
to soften beyond strategic self-management—these states threaten the curated self. [^Illouz]

The cultural pattern becomes visible not only in theory but in lived biographies.
As Foucault has demonstrated, sexuality is never merely instinctual;
it is organised through discursive norms that define what counts as legitimate desire.[^FoucaultHistoryOfSex]

The lifeline of our case person exemplifies this.
They stood faithfully in an essentially non-functional relationship, raising children, for three decades.
The point is that they thought that to be exemplary and desired.
Indeed, according to their report, they thought the relationship to function normally,
sexuality being such a dull repetitive performance as they experienced it to be,
as they possessed no capacity even to imagine that a man could receive.

Their body responses illustrate how the body does not easily distinguish between relational exposure and structural vulnerability.
For them, receptivity was culturally framed as something that does not even exist, something that could not be imagined.
It is no wonder that once they started to see their life in a wider perspective and sexuality beyond limited performance
became possible, their body registered erotic opening as a danger.
The crucial point here is that our case person _could not even imagine receptivity, due to their embodied cultural conditioning._
We strongly suspect that this configuration is not rare in our society.

At the same time, of course, performance logics may and do coexist with genuine intimacy;
the claim here is structural selectivity, not universal corruption.
Indeed, we surmise that panic at the threshold of receiving does not typically signal fear of sexuality itself.
More often, it may signal fear of dissolving into a role that the culture has not legitimised.

Summarising, modern erotic scripts are built around agency and competence
while receiving requires relinquishing such strategic self-management.
In our culture, only strategic self-management is rewarded.
This appears to be a core reason why deep receiving threatens identity coherence,
leading to persona configurations where deep receiving is not even imaginable.

This is not (yet) an indictment.
At this point, we are making an observation of structural selectivity.
When sexuality is narrated primarily through agency, performance, and choice,
the grammar for being received — without self-loss, without debt, without subordination —
necessarily remains comparatively underdeveloped within a culture.

### Receiving without self-loss: a missing category

If in the preceding sections we have traced
the narrowing of cultural space for dependency and the performance-formatting of intimacy,
we now arrive at a more precise gap.

Modern Western cultures possess a rich symbolic vocabulary for:

- self-assertion,
- self-sacrifice,
- self-transcendence,
- self-mastery.

They are comparatively thin, however, when it comes to a more delicate configuration: **receiving while remaining a subject**.

Receiving is frequently framed as one of four things:

- regression (a return to childish need),
- indulgence (unearned pleasure),
- surrender (loss of agency),
- or spiritual bypass (premature dissolution of individuality).

What is poorly symbolised is a state in which one can soften, be affected,
and remain relationally open without collapsing into subordination or erasure.

From a developmental perspective, this absence is not trivial.
Winnicott’s notion of the *holding environment* and Bion’s formulation of *container and contained* both
imply that early receptivity becomes safe only when experience can be metabolised without overwhelming the self.
What is internalised in healthy development is not permanent control,
but a confidence in reversibility — the sense that intense states can be entered and exited without loss of continuity.

Across contemplative traditions, a parallel caution appears.
Intense openings — whether devotional, erotic, or mystical — are described as potentially destabilising and
therefore requiring guidance, containment, or gradual training.
This convergence suggests that the nervous system does not tolerate radical deorganisation without sufficient relational or structural support.
What myth encodes as covenant and rescue protocol, and psychology describes as co-regulation and titration,
may be pointing to the same requirement: deep descent must remain reversible.
Even contemporary cultural language rarely names this intermediate possibility.
We assert, sacrifice, and transcend.
The category of *receiving without self-loss* remains comparatively underdeveloped.

Structurally, when such a category is missing, two consequences follow.

First, receptivity becomes neurologically risky.
If openness is culturally associated with weakness, debt, or disappearance,
the body may treat sustained softness as exposure to threat.
Defensive organisations around control or shutdown then appear not as pathology,
but as boundary enforcement.

Second, at scale, this dynamic feeds back into culture.
When receptivity is experienced as dangerous,
individuals organise around action, performance, and control.
Aggregated across many subjects, this stabilises social forms
that further privilege control-heavy modes of regulation.
The absence of a symbol for safe receiving thus reinforces
the very structures that make receiving perilous.

In this sense, the problem is not simply therapeutic or medical but cultural.
A society that lacks a liveable image of receiving without self-loss
leaves its members oscillating between domination and subordination.
Over time, this oscillation does not merely shape individual symptoms;
it reshapes behavioural expectations at scale.

When control becomes the primary guarantor of coherence,
relational life reorganises around vigilance, hierarchy, and pre-emptive defence. [^Elias]
What began as a defensive adaptation in individual nervous systems can sediment into collective style. [^Bourdieu]
The logic of the sovereign's hierarchy becomes embodied —
not only in institutions, but in posture, tone, sexual scripts, and conflict patterns. [^Foucault]

Speaking figuratively, one could say that
under such conditions erotic, affiliative humans begin to resemble competitive pack animals.
This is not a biological claim, but a structural one:
cultural grammars of control can redirect attachment energy away from mutual regulation and toward dominance calibration.
Where witnessing is thin, alliance replaces intimacy, and rank replaces reciprocity.

In this way, culture does not merely constrain behaviour;
it sculpts it deeply enough that shifts can appear almost species-level in magnitude.
The change is not genetic but symbolic and regulatory.
Yet, its effects are visible in how bodies pair, how groups form, and how vulnerability is negotiated.

The loop tightens.

[^Elias: Elias, Norbert. _The civilizing process._ (1939)]

### When bodies do what culture cannot name

Bodies do not wait for cultural permission to regulate.

From a developmental and neurobiological perspective, receiving is not an optional refinement;
it is part of the regulatory infrastructure of mammalian life.
Co-regulation precedes self-regulation.
Safety is encoded through repeated experiences of being held in attention without overwhelm. [^Winnicott] [^Bion]
Polyvagal formulations make the same point in physiological language:
ventral states of social engagement depend on cues of safety that are relationally mediated. [^Porges] [^Dana]

When a culture lacks a stable symbolic grammar for receiving without self-loss,
this thoroughly biological requirement does not disappear. It becomes privatised.

Instead of being narrated, it is enacted.
Instead of being legitimised, it is somatised.

Panic, numbness, and dissociation may then appear, not as rebellion or dysfunction, but as boundary enforcement.
If sustained openness is experienced as debt, capture, or evaluation, defensive contraction becomes adaptive.
As trauma literature repeatedly shows,
the nervous system prioritises survival over coherence of identity. [^vanDerKolk] [^Levine]
The refusal of receptivity is often the preservation of integrity under incompatible conditions.

At scale, this dynamic remains largely invisible.
Symptoms are treated individually, medicalised, or moralised, [^Foucault]
while the cultural absence that rendered them necessary remains unnamed.

It shall be noted that
we are not claiming contemporary cultures cause trauma in any simple or monocausal sense.
We are merely observing a structural mismatch: bodies require forms of reversible openness;
cultural-symbolic orders variably support or destabilise them.
Where support is thin, regulation migrates underground and disappears from sight.

What culture cannot name, bodies will nevertheless perform.

### Gender roles as control-distribution systems

Gender systems can be read, among other things,
as historical mechanisms for distributing control, vulnerability, and risk.

Across many traditional configurations, roles have organised who acts and who receives,
who penetrates and who is penetrated, who carries economic exposure and who carries relational exposure.
These arrangements have varied widely across cultures and epochs,
yet they frequently stabilise a similar polarity:
agency is coded upward, receptivity downward, within an accountability structure. [^WestZimmermann]

Someone must manage; someone must be managed.
Someone must absorb risk; someone must demonstrate competence.
The polarity may shift bodies, but the template can remain.

From the perspective developed in this essay, the relevance of gender roles is structural rather than ideological.
If receptivity is culturally coded as subordinate, then receiving without self-loss becomes difficult to symbolise.
If masculinity is historically tethered to autonomy and impermeability,
then male receptivity easily reads as surrender rather than relational depth.
Conversely, if femininity is tethered to nurturance and accommodation,
then agency can appear as deviation rather than integration.

Giddens argues that late modernity loosens fixed gender scripts. [^Giddens]
Yet our society does not eliminate performance pressures.
Illouz has similarly shown how romantic life has become formatted by evaluation and comparison [^Illouz].
While the old scripts may be destabilising, the grammar of control is not automatically disappearing.

Many contemporary conflicts around gender and sexuality can therefore be read
as _disputes over where control may soften and where vulnerability may remain_ a subject rather than a role.
For us, the gender turbulence we observe in our society does not signal mere confusion,
but can therefore be read as a structural renegotiation of who is permitted to receive without forfeiting legitimacy.

We do not claim that gender is reducible to control distribution,
nor that all traditions function identically.
We make a narrower observation:
gender systems have historically organised the traffic between doing and receiving.
When those systems strain or dissolve, the problem of witnessed receptivity becomes newly visible.

[^WestZimmermann: West, Candace, and Don H. Zimmerman. "Doing gender." _Gender & society 1.2 (1987): 125-151._]

## Witnessed Receptivity Defined

We have now moved across myth, development, and culture, assembling the pillars one by one.
The turn now is architectural rather than exploratory: not another image, but the span that binds them.
What follows does not introduce a new theme.
It names, in concentrated form, the structure that has been implicit all along.

**Witnessed receptivity** is the capacity to open, soften, and be affected while retaining continuity of being.
It is not simply receiving; it is receiving within a relational or internalised field that secures reversibility.
In this configuration, descent does not equal annihilation, exposure does not equal capture, and surrender does not abolish return.
The defining feature is not control, but embodied confidence that openness will not dissolve subjecthood.

Witnessed receptivity is therefore not submission, not regression, and not sacrificial self-erasure.
It does not bind the receiver into debt, hierarchy, or incorporation under a sovereign gaze.
It differs equally from defensive autonomy: armour preserves the self by contraction; stone preserves it by disappearance.
Witnessed receptivity preserves it by relation.
Nor is it identical with contemplative self-transcendence;
non-dual states may arise from it, but they do not replace its developmental ground.

The myths we have traced each articulated a necessary operator: covenant before descent,
witnessing without capture, agency without domination, re-membering after fragmentation,
and the ever-present temptation of crisis-logic.
Witnessed receptivity is the structural integration of these elements.
It names the condition under which deep receiving becomes survivable because return is encoded as possible.

We require this concept because our dominant symbolic vocabularies are thin at precisely this point.
Autonomy, mastery, sacrifice, transcendence — these we name fluently.
Receiving without self-loss, we do not.
Where that grammar is absent, bodies oscillate between control and collapse,
and cultures mistake defensive organisation for strength.
Witnessed receptivity names the missing middle posture: openness that does not cost the self.

### Developmental arc: from external witness to structural reversibility

The structure of witnessed receptivity unfolds developmentally.
There is a small risk of it being misunderstood, if “witnessing” is imagined as
a permanent internal observer that must remain active during deep surrender.
This is **not** our claim.

To clarify the developmental trajectory, we distinguish several stages:

- **Stage 1: External witnessing**
  - Receptivity is stabilised through the reliable presence of an actual physical other.
  - Co-regulation precedes self-regulation.
  - Descent (into affect, intimacy, bodily openness) is survivable because return is relationally secured.

- **Stage 2: Internalisation of reversibility**
  - What becomes internalised is not an observing ego or any other conscious part/function,
    but a structural/neural confidence that collapse does not equal annihilation.
  - The nervous system encodes that intense states can be entered and exited without loss of continuity.
  - Return becomes increasingly automatic, similar to how breathing resumes after a held exhalation (bahya kumbhaka).
  - No active external or internal “watcher” is required in the moment of deep surrender;
    what is required is embodied confidence in return.

- **Stage 3 (possible but not foundational): Non-subjective awareness**
  - In some contemplative traditions and advanced experiential states,
    experience may occur without a clearly felt subject–object structure
    (“experience without experiencer”).
  - Such states are phenomenologically distinct from dissociation or collapse.
  - However, they presuppose an already integrated system capable of reversibility.
  - Non-dual or non-subjective awareness does not replace developmental witnessing;
    it emerges from a system in which receptivity has already become survivable.

This distinction guards against two confusions:

- Confusing trauma-related dissociation with contemplative self-transcendence.
- Reintroducing subtle control in the form of an internal monitor that prevents full surrender.

For the purposes of this essay, the central claim remains developmentally grounded:

> Deep receiving becomes safe not because someone remains in control,
> but because the system has learned that openness does not lead to annihilation.

Whether the non-subjective witnessing described in contemplative traditions
should be treated as a further stage of integration,
a different ontological claim,
or a distinct phenomenological domain,
is left open for later exploration and remains beyond the scope of the present essay.

The preceding sections have moved between developmental psychology, somatic regulation, and mythic structure. Before turning to practical implications, we briefly record a more speculative synthesis that emerged in dialogue during the writing of this essay. It should be read strictly as a hypothesis and not as an established conclusion.

Across trauma collapse, psychedelic destabilisation, and reports of contemplative ego-dissolution, there appears to be a recurring structural motif: a loosening or reorganisation of the ordinary self-model. The phenomenology varies dramatically — from panic and fragmentation to clarity and non-dual openness — yet a shared dynamic may be at work.

One candidate "core" for this dynamic can be formulated cautiously as follows:

> the capacity to maintain *just enough multi-level coherence* while prediction/precision regimes reorganise.

Here, "multi-level coherence" refers not to rigid control, but to a minimal continuity across bodily regulation, affect, attention, and narrative orientation. If coherence collapses too far, the result may resemble panic, dissociation, or psychotic decompensation. If coherence remains too rigid, reorganisation does not occur and defensive structures remain intact. Transformation appears possible only within a narrow corridor: enough loosening to allow reconfiguration, yet enough stability to permit return.

In mythic language, this corridor is encoded as the covenant before descent (Inanna), the hearing field that does not capture (Avalokiteśvara), and the labour of re-membering (Lemminkäinen’s mother; Isis). In developmental language, it resembles the internalisation of a witnessing function that makes openness survivable. In somatic language, it may correspond to preserved regulatory anchoring even as identity structures soften.

We emphasise that this formulation remains speculative. The present essay does not attempt to demonstrate a single underlying mechanism across trauma, psychosis, contemplative experience, and cultural symbolism. It merely observes that the requirement of reversibility — descent that does not become annihilation — recurs across these domains. Whether this convergence points to a shared biological constraint, a structural feature of consciousness, or a limitation of our descriptive frameworks remains an open question.

Nevertheless, the hypothesis offers a unifying lens for the argument advanced so far: witnessed receptivity may function as one cultural and developmental strategy for preserving "just enough" coherence when self-structures reorganise. Without such preservation, openness risks collapse; with it, reorganisation can become integration rather than fragmentation.

The implications of this hypothesis — for trauma work, sexuality, contemplative practice, and cultural imagination — are substantial but cannot be developed fully here. They remain directions for further work.

## Societal level — working note on asymmetrical attention and the politics of receiving (draft)

### Sovereign attention and the danger of being seen (working draft)

The developmental pattern described above may have a structural analogue at the societal level.

Across many mythic and historical contexts, being *seen* by a sovereign — king, ruler, divine authority — is framed as both elevation and danger.
Similarly, receiving a gift from the sovereign often implies not simple generosity, but incorporation into hierarchy, debt, or obligation.
Anthropological accounts of gift exchange (e.g. Mauss) show that gifts are rarely neutral: to receive is to enter a binding relation.

This motif clarifies a possible cultural layer of the trauma around receptivity:

- Attention may be experienced not as mutual witnessing but as asymmetrical evaluation.
- Receiving may be encoded not as nourishment but as debt.
- Care may implicitly demand gratitude, loyalty, productivity, or compliance.
- Visibility may collapse subjecthood into role.

At the societal scale, we may therefore need to distinguish between two structurally different forms of attention:

- **Sovereign attention**
  - Vertical, evaluative, binding.
  - Produces obligation and incorporation.
  - Can destabilise the receiver’s autonomy.

- **Witnessing attention**
  - Horizontal, non-extractive, non-binding.
  - Preserves subjecthood.
  - Allows receiving without debt.

If cultural narratives and institutions privilege the sovereign model of attention — reward, performance, recognition, visibility, productivity — then receptivity may become politically ambiguous.
To receive is to risk subordination.

This distinction may later support a broader societal argument:
that cultures organised around performance, domination, and crisis-efficiency
risk reinforcing defensive organisations that equate openness with capture.

This note is intentionally provisional.
It marks a direction for later development:
linking trauma dynamics, mythic operators, and political structure
through the shared problem of asymmetrical attention.

### Inanna and Ninshubur — a brief account of the myth

Inanna, goddess of heaven, fertility, and sovereignty, decides to descend to the underworld,
the realm of her sister Ereshkigal.
The reasons for the descent vary by version, but the act itself is deliberate and chosen.

Before leaving, Inanna prepares carefully.
She appoints her loyal attendant Ninshubur and gives her explicit instructions:
if Inanna does not return after a fixed period,
Ninshubur must seek help from the gods and demand her release.
Only after establishing this arrangement does Inanna set out.

At the gates of the underworld, Inanna is required to pass through seven gates.
At each gate she is stripped of one item of clothing or regalia —
symbols of her power, status, and identity —
until she enters the underworld naked and without authority.

Before Ereshkigal and the judges of the underworld,
Inanna is accused of violating the laws of the Great Below.
In the underworld there is no privilege, no status, and no exception:
whoever enters must submit to its order.

Inanna’s presence is judged as an act of transgression.
She has crossed the boundary between worlds while still carrying
the residue of life, power, and sovereignty.
For this, she is pronounced guilty according to the ancient laws of the underworld.

Ereshkigal fixes her gaze upon Inanna and speaks the word of judgement.
The Anunna, the judges of the underworld, pronounce the sentence.
Inanna is struck down, killed, and her lifeless body is hung on a hook,
exposed and unmourned, as one who has lost all name, power, and claim.

After the allotted time has passed, Ninshubur follows the instructions she was given.
She goes first to Enlil and then to Nanna, both of whom refuse to intervene.
Finally she reaches Enki, the god of wisdom.
Enki fashions two small beings from dirt beneath his fingernails and
sends them to the underworld with the food and water of life.

Ereshkigal is in anguish from the pains of the underworld itself:
she suffers alone, bearing the weight of death, loss, and separation.
Her cries are those of one who rules a realm of endings
yet receives no comfort, no witness, and no relief.
She labours in sorrow without companionship,
as queen of a place where none return.

Instead of confronting or commanding her,
Enki's emissaries listen to her cries and mirror her pain.
Pleased by their empathy, Ereshkigal offers them a gift.
They ask for Inanna’s body, which is given to them.
With the food and water of life, they revive Inanna.

However, Inanna cannot leave the underworld freely.
She is told that someone must take her place.
As she ascends, demons accompany her, seeking a substitute.
Inanna spares those who have mourned her absence, including Ninshubur,
but eventually chooses her husband Dumuzi,
who is found sitting comfortably on his throne and not grieving.
Dumuzi is taken to the underworld for part of the year,
while his sister Geshtinanna later shares the burden,
allowing each to spend part of the year below and part above.

Thus Inanna returns to the world of the living,
transformed by the descent,
while the cycle of descent and return
becomes woven into the order of life and seasons.

### Tārā — a brief account of the myth

In Buddhist tradition,
Tārā is a bodhisattva associated with compassion, protection, and swift response to suffering.
Her origins are told in several ways across Indian and Tibetan sources.

In one widely known account,
Tārā arises from the tears of Avalokiteśvara,
the bodhisattva of compassion.
As Avalokiteśvara gazes upon the suffering of countless beings and hears their cries,
tears fall from his eyes.
From these tears, Tārā is born, embodying compassion that takes active form.

In other traditions,
Tārā is described as a princess
who vows to attain awakening and to work for the liberation of all beings.
When advised that she should pray to be reborn as a man in future lives, she refuses.
Instead, she vows to remain in female form until awakening is complete,
declaring that enlightenment is not dependent on gender.

Tārā is often depicted as alert and ready to act, one leg extended as if about to rise.
She is invoked as a protector who responds quickly to danger, fear, and distress.
Numerous forms of Tārā exist, including peaceful and fierce manifestations,
each associated with specific obstacles or sufferings.

Across these tellings, Tārā is remembered as one who hears suffering and responds,
not by withdrawal or domination,
but by compassionate action that preserves life and alleviates fear.
Her activity is continuous and relational,
directed toward beings as they are,
in the midst of danger rather than beyond it.

### Isis and Osiris — a brief account of the myth

In ancient Egyptian mythology,
Osiris is a divine king associated with fertility, agriculture, and just rule.
He brings order to the land through continuity and renewal rather than conquest.
His brother Seth, associated with disorder, violence, and rivalry, grows hostile toward him.

Seth plots against Osiris and murders him,
in some versions by sealing him in a chest and casting it into the Nile.
After Osiris’ death, Seth dismembers his body,
cutting it into pieces and scattering them across Egypt
to prevent his restoration and to secure his own power.

Isis, the wife of Osiris, sets out to search for the scattered parts.
She travels throughout the land, gathering each fragment of Osiris’ body.
With the help of magic and devotion, she reassembles him.
One part, however — the phallus — cannot be recovered, having been lost or destroyed.

Because Osiris cannot be fully restored to embodied life,
he does not return to rule among the living.
Instead, he becomes lord of the underworld, ruling the realm of the dead.
Before withdrawing from the world, Isis fashions a substitute phallus and conceives a son, Horus.

Horus grows to challenge Seth for kingship.
Their conflict is prolonged and complex, involving trials, contests, and judgement by the gods.

In the so-called *lettuce incident*, Seth later attempts to assert dominance over Horus
through a sexual act intended to establish superiority.
In Egyptian mythic logic, semen is a marker of power and legitimacy.
Seth secretly places his semen on lettuce, a food associated with vitality,
and arranges for Horus to eat it.

Horus, however, has already been protected by Isis.
Instead of Seth’s semen entering Horus’ body,
Horus’ own semen is placed upon Seth.
When the gods later call forth the semen to determine dominance,
Seth’s semen fails to answer, while Horus’ responds from within Seth.

The judgement exposes Seth’s attempt at domination as hollow.
Power sought through coercion and un-witnessed sexuality
is publicly revealed as illegitimate,
and Seth’s claim to superiority collapses.

In the end, Horus is recognised as the rightful heir,
restoring order among the living, while Osiris remains ruler of the underworld.

Through Isis’ labour, continuity is preserved, but the prior order is not fully restored.
The myth thus links death, dismemberment, reassembly, and lineage into a single cycle,
in which life continues only through transformation rather than return.
