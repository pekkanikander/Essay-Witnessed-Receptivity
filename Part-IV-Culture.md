## Receiving in the culture of Control

What we have seen above has prepared the ground for examining our contemporary Western culture,
without kings yet saturated with evaluation.  While there are wider considerations (as we just saw),
here we focus on how our culture organises and fails to organise receiving,
at the symbolic, archetypal, and psychological (trauma) levels.
Above we outlined the structural risks when a control culture takes its most condensed and radical forms.
We now examine the everyday cultural conditions in which such dynamics either intensify or soften.

We ask a broad question: to which extend do our modern Western cultures possess
adequate symbolic, relational, and institutional supports for witnessed receptivity,
and how much the new emerging forms of identity and embodiment
are attempts to solve a problem that the existing structures can no longer hold.

### Control as pervasive background assumption

Compared to many other cultures, our contemporary western culture may be characterised as a culture of control.
The strong and wide control structures have brought us a lot (mostly material) good;
they have made possible such marvels as global trade and unforeseen level of material affluence.
At the same time, it has framed — as cultures do — what it means to be a human and good citizen.
It has created a normative structure that defines not only the values everyone is supposed to share
but also a multitude of institutions that are tactly assumed to be real and perpetual.

The role of control as a default cultural virtue emphasises
self-regulation, self-management, self-optimisation.
Adult autonomy is framed as independence from need. Every man is the architect of his own fortune.
Loss of control is framed as failure, immaturity, or danger, deeply receiving as a state that inherently threatens control.

In our culture, control is not only a virtue but an ambient norm.
Any adult overtly losing control, even temporarily, is considered impolite at minimum and lunatic at the other end,
depending on the depth and length of losing control.

A culture of control is not only a set of values; it is produced and tactly enforced by institutional pressures.
In Foucault’s terms, modern power operates no more by sovereign command but through discipline and normalisation:
the man learns to supervise himself and to equate autonomy with self-management. [^Foucault]
Rosa’s analysis of social acceleration clarifies why this supervision becomes pervasive:
under conditions of dynamic stabilisation, speed and efficiency are moralised because falling behind is structurally punished. [^Rosa]

Illouz shows how these logics enter intimacy itself:
romance and sexuality tend to become organised through choice, performance,
and evaluative comparison, narrowing the symbolic space for being affected without role pressure. [^Illouz]
Luhmann’s account of functional differentiation adds a final twist:
modern society lacks a shared public code for “receiving while remaining a subject”,
so difficulties of receptivity are privatised—medicalised, psychologised, or pushed into self-help—rather than culturally held. [^Luhmann]
Under such conditions, the body’s refusal of preceived openness appears as pathologisable symptom
instead of a somatic boundary enforcement against incompatible demands.

[^Rosa: Rosa, Hartmut. _Social acceleration: A new theory of modernity._ Columbia University Press, 2013.]

[^Illouz: Illouz, Eva. _Cold intimacies: The making of emotional capitalism._ Polity, 2007.]

### The Culture of Control: Everyday Observations

If one wished to observe the culture of control in its natural habitat,
one would not need to attend a military parade.
One could simply wake up on a Tuesday.

The day begins not with sunrise but with an alarm —
a small sovereign device that informs us that our body’s rhythms are, while interesting, secondary.
We do not wake because we are rested. We wake because the schedule has spoken.

Before standing up, many of us consult a screen.
The screen presents a curated list of obligations: emails, tasks, meetings, reminders.
It is, in effect, a quiet roll call.
One confirms attendance not by speaking, but by replying, scheduling, and optimising.

Breakfast is often taken not because hunger slowly ripens, but because time allows for seven minutes.
The commute is calculated. The route is optimised. Even leisure is pre-booked, tracked, and evaluated.
If one goes for a walk, one may measure steps. If one meditates, one may track minutes.
If one sleeps, one may review the data the next morning to determine whether one has slept correctly.

Control is rarely experienced as oppression. It is experienced as responsibility.

We manage calendars, bodies, relationships, finances, reputations.
We respond to notifications promptly because delay feels vaguely immoral.
We apologise for taking time.
We describe ourselves as “behind” even when no one is chasing us directly.

It is difficult to identify where the self ends and the ambient norm begins.
We discipline ourselves in order to remain employable, attractive, efficient, sane.

Public loss of control is handled with discomfort. Tears in a workplace meeting require explanation.
Anger must be reframed as “passion.”

Even intimacy is not entirely exempt.
Romantic life is organised through profiles, comparison, selection criteria.
Attraction is filtered through self-presentation.
One speaks of “working on oneself,” “improving communication,” “being emotionally available” —
all admirable aims, yet phrased as projects requiring management.

None of this is sinister. It has produced extraordinary coordination, prosperity, and safety.
It allows millions of strangers to cooperate without chaos.

Yet it establishes a background assumption: adulthood is maintaining functional control.
To be a competent subject is to regulate oneself continuously.

Within such an atmosphere, experiences that require loosening control,
e.g. grief that does not follow a schedule or erotic receptivity that cannot be optimised,
appear suspicious.
They resist measurement. They cannot be easily entered into a calendar.

The culture does not forbid such states. It simply fails to provide them with a stable public grammar.

As a result, when the body hesitates before deep receiving, the hesitation does not appear culturally legible.
It appears inefficient, regressive, and especially embarrassing.

The sovereign has not disappeared. It has been miniaturised and internalised.
It now travels in our pockets and speaks in our own voice.

And so the day proceeds: controlled, mostly by ourselves, and subtly, almost politely, mandated from everywhere.

### Efficiency, speed, and the moralisation of action

Growth and efficiency are two pillars of our capitalist value system.
Modern economies rely on growth and efficiency for stability.

At the everyday level, the economic system and the cultural norms
force us to increase efficiency, to work harder and faster.
This applies more-or-less to everyone, from the unemployed who is supposed
submit hundreds of applications to receive benefits to the superrich
who work 16 hours a day, though supposedly because they just like it.

In our culture, speed and efficiency are treated as moral goods, not just practical ones.
Slowness, hesitation, and waiting are framed as wasteful or indulgent.
According to our norms, a slow person is a lazy one, making it morally acceptable for them to fall into poverty.
The norm narrates this as their deliberate choice, independent of the actual circumstances.

We have a strong cultural bias for preferring doing over being, acting over sensing, and producing over receiving.

Under cultural acceleration, time becomes a scarce moral resource;
experiences that require unhurried attunement—care,
erotic receiving, grief, integration—are pressured into efficiency formats,
and the nervous system responds with shutdown or panic when forced past its tolerances. [^Rosa]

This raises an implicit question: What happens to forms of experience whose regulation depends on slowness, repetition, and reversible exposure?

### The narrowing of acceptable dependency

Modern Western cultures have not eliminated dependency; they have tightly zoned it.

Dependency is widely tolerated and even expected in infancy.
It is temporarily legitimised in acute illness.
It is forgiven in moments of recognised catastrophe.
However, outside these clearly demarcated zones,
sustained dependency is considered culturally suspicious or at least ambiguous.

Adult needs are expected to be brief, goal-oriented, and controlled. [^Rosa]
An adult may require help, but one must also demonstrate progress.
Therapy is acceptable, but only if it leads to improvement.
Grief is legitimate, provided it does not last “too long.”
Financial support is defensible, provided it is framed as temporary.
Even friendships often operate under the tacit assumptions that reciprocity must remain roughly balanced and visibly so.

In this structure, dependency becomes easily medicalised, psychologised, or moralised. [^Foucault]
If one cannot function independently, the problem is diagnosed.
If one relies too heavily on another, the relation is perceived as co-dependency.
If one remains attached, the issue is reframed as immaturity. [^Honneth]

The ensuing difficulties for witnessed receptivity are subtle but consequential.
Deep receiving resembles dependency when perceived through prevailing cultural norms.
It involves softening, leaning, and allowing oneself to be affected.
Earlier we traced how, in development, repeated experiences of misattuned or conditional care
can lead the nervous system to equate leaning with risk.
When dependency is culturally framed as deficit or immaturity, that early learning finds confirmation.
The body may therefore register sustained openness not as connection but as potential regression,
and remaining in need not as relational depth but as exposure to judgement.

Deep receiving, therefore, has no stable cultural home.
It is either becomes compressed into exceptional states or redirected into self-sufficiency.
The result is that ordinary, mutual vulnerability becomes structurally under-supported.

[^Honneth: Honneth, Axel. _The struggle for recognition: The moral grammar of social conflicts._ MIT press, 1996.]

### Sexuality under performance norms

Intimacy, too, does not escape these pressures.
In contemporary Western contexts, sexuality is increasingly organised through
the logics of performance, optimisation, and evaluation.
Desire is discussed in terms of arousal levels. Encounters are measured through satisfaction.
Technique, responsiveness, and outcome become implicit benchmarks. [^Illouz]
Of course, none of this is inherently pathological. For example, clear communication and attentiveness can deepen connection.
The difficulty arises when _sexuality is subtly formatted as a site of achievement._

Under performance norms, erotic life risks becoming an arena in which one must succeed.
The emphasis falls on doing well—being attentive, confident, competent—rather than on being affected.
Even the language for surrendering into sensation, for being held without agenda,
for receiving without role pressure remains comparatively thin. [^Giddens]

Romantic capitalism intensifies this formatting.
Intimate partners are selected through profiles, compared across criteria, and evaluated according to perceived value.
The self becomes a project of presentation and continuous refinement.
Within such an economy, receptive dependency can resemble loss of market-agency.
To be overtaken by desire, to need without calculation,
to soften beyond strategic self-management—these states threaten the curated self. [^Illouz]

The cultural pattern becomes visible not only in theory but in lived biographies.
As Foucault has demonstrated, sexuality is never merely instinctual;
it is organised through discursive norms that define what counts as legitimate desire.[^FoucaultHistoryOfSex]

The lifeline of our case person exemplifies this.
They stood faithfully in an essentially non-functional relationship, raising children, for three decades.
The point is that they thought that to be exemplary and desired.
Indeed, according to their report, they thought the relationship to function normally,
sexuality being such a dull repetitive performance as they experienced it to be,
as they possessed no capacity even to imagine that a man could receive.

Their body responses illustrate how the body does not easily distinguish between relational exposure and structural vulnerability.
For them, receptivity was culturally framed as something that does not even exist, something that could not be imagined.
It is no wonder that once they started to see their life in a wider perspective and sexuality beyond limited performance
became possible, their body registered erotic opening as a danger.
The crucial point here is that our case person _could not even imagine receptivity, due to their embodied cultural conditioning._
We strongly suspect that this configuration is not rare in our society.

At the same time, of course, performance logics may and do coexist with genuine intimacy;
the claim here is structural selectivity, not universal corruption.
Indeed, we surmise that panic at the threshold of receiving does not typically signal fear of sexuality itself.
More often, it may signal fear of dissolving into a role that the culture has not legitimised.

Summarising, modern erotic scripts are built around agency and competence
while receiving requires relinquishing such strategic self-management.
In our culture, only strategic self-management is rewarded.
This appears to be a core reason why deep receiving threatens identity coherence,
leading to persona configurations where deep receiving is not even imaginable.

This is not (yet) an indictment.
At this point, we are making an observation of structural selectivity.
When sexuality is narrated primarily through agency, performance, and choice,
the grammar for being received — without self-loss, without debt, without subordination —
necessarily remains comparatively underdeveloped within a culture.

[^Giddens: Giddens, Anthony. _The Transformation of Intimacy: Sexuality, Love and Eroticism in Modern Societies._ Stanford University Press, 1992]

[^FoucaultHistoryOfSex: Foucault, Michel. _The history of sexuality: An introduction._ Vintage, 1990.]

### Receiving without self-loss: a missing category

If in the preceding sections we have traced
the narrowing of cultural space for dependency and the performance-formatting of intimacy,
we now arrive at a more precise gap.

Modern Western cultures possess a rich symbolic vocabulary for:

- self-assertion,
- self-sacrifice,
- self-transcendence,
- self-mastery.

They are comparatively thin, however, when it comes to a more delicate configuration: **receiving while remaining a subject**.

Receiving is frequently framed as one of four things:

- regression (a return to childish need),
- indulgence (unearned pleasure),
- surrender (loss of agency),
- or spiritual bypass (premature dissolution of individuality).

What is poorly symbolised is a state in which one can soften, be affected,
and remain relationally open without collapsing into subordination or erasure.

From a developmental perspective, this absence is not trivial.
Winnicott’s notion of the *holding environment* and Bion’s formulation of *container and contained* both
imply that early receptivity becomes safe only when experience can be metabolised without overwhelming the self.
What is internalised in healthy development is not permanent control,
but a confidence in reversibility — the sense that intense states can be entered and exited without loss of continuity.

Across contemplative traditions, a parallel caution appears.
Intense openings — whether devotional, erotic, or mystical — are described as potentially destabilising and
therefore requiring guidance, containment, or gradual training.
This convergence suggests that the nervous system does not tolerate radical deorganisation without sufficient relational or structural support.
What myth encodes as covenant and rescue protocol, and psychology describes as co-regulation and titration,
may be pointing to the same requirement: deep descent must remain reversible.
Even contemporary cultural language rarely names this intermediate possibility.
We assert, sacrifice, and transcend.
The category of *receiving without self-loss* remains comparatively underdeveloped.

Structurally, when such a category is missing, two consequences follow.

First, receptivity becomes neurologically risky.
If openness is culturally associated with weakness, debt, or disappearance,
the body may treat sustained softness as exposure to threat.
Defensive organisations around control or shutdown then appear not as pathology,
but as boundary enforcement.

Second, at scale, this dynamic feeds back into culture.
When receptivity is experienced as dangerous,
individuals organise around action, performance, and control.
Aggregated across many subjects, this stabilises social forms
that further privilege control-heavy modes of regulation.
The absence of a symbol for safe receiving thus reinforces
the very structures that make receiving perilous.

In this sense, the problem is not simply therapeutic or medical but cultural.
A society that lacks a liveable image of receiving without self-loss
leaves its members oscillating between domination and subordination.
Over time, this oscillation does not merely shape individual symptoms;
it reshapes behavioural expectations at scale.

When control becomes the primary guarantor of coherence,
relational life reorganises around vigilance, hierarchy, and pre-emptive defence. [^Elias]
What began as a defensive adaptation in individual nervous systems can sediment into collective style. [^Bourdieu]
The logic of the sovereign's hierarchy becomes embodied —
not only in institutions, but in posture, tone, sexual scripts, and conflict patterns. [^Foucault]

Speaking figuratively, one could say that
under such conditions erotic, affiliative humans begin to resemble competitive pack animals.
This is not a biological claim, but a structural one:
cultural grammars of control can redirect attachment energy away from mutual regulation and toward dominance calibration.
Where witnessing is thin, alliance replaces intimacy, and rank replaces reciprocity.

In this way, culture does not merely constrain behaviour;
it sculpts it deeply enough that shifts can appear almost species-level in magnitude.
The change is not genetic but symbolic and regulatory.
Yet, its effects are visible in how bodies pair, how groups form, and how vulnerability is negotiated.

The loop tightens.

[^Elias: Elias, Norbert. _The civilizing process._ (1939)]

### When bodies do what culture cannot name

Bodies do not wait for cultural permission to regulate.

From a developmental and neurobiological perspective, receiving is not an optional refinement;
it is part of the regulatory infrastructure of mammalian life.
Co-regulation precedes self-regulation.
Safety is encoded through repeated experiences of being held in attention without overwhelm. [^Winnicott] [^Bion]
Polyvagal formulations make the same point in physiological language:
ventral states of social engagement depend on cues of safety that are relationally mediated. [^Porges] [^Dana]

When a culture lacks a stable symbolic grammar for receiving without self-loss,
this thoroughly biological requirement does not disappear. It becomes privatised.

Instead of being narrated, it is enacted.
Instead of being legitimised, it is somatised.

Panic, numbness, and dissociation may then appear, not as rebellion or dysfunction, but as boundary enforcement.
If sustained openness is experienced as debt, capture, or evaluation, defensive contraction becomes adaptive.
As trauma literature repeatedly shows,
the nervous system prioritises survival over coherence of identity. [^vanDerKolk] [^Levine]
The refusal of receptivity is often the preservation of integrity under incompatible conditions.

At scale, this dynamic remains largely invisible.
Symptoms are treated individually, medicalised, or moralised, [^Foucault]
while the cultural absence that rendered them necessary remains unnamed.

It shall be noted that
we are not claiming contemporary cultures cause trauma in any simple or monocausal sense.
We are merely observing a structural mismatch: bodies require forms of reversible openness;
cultural-symbolic orders variably support or destabilise them.
Where support is thin, regulation migrates underground and disappears from sight.

What culture cannot name, bodies will nevertheless perform.

### Gender roles as control-distribution systems

Gender systems can be read, among other things,
as historical mechanisms for distributing control, vulnerability, and risk.

Across many traditional configurations, roles have organised who acts and who receives,
who penetrates and who is penetrated, who carries economic exposure and who carries relational exposure.
These arrangements have varied widely across cultures and epochs,
yet they frequently stabilise a similar polarity:
agency is coded upward, receptivity downward, within an accountability structure. [^WestZimmermann]

Someone must manage; someone must be managed.
Someone must absorb risk; someone must demonstrate competence.
The polarity may shift bodies, but the template can remain.

From the perspective developed in this essay, the relevance of gender roles is structural rather than ideological.
If receptivity is culturally coded as subordinate, then receiving without self-loss becomes difficult to symbolise.
If masculinity is historically tethered to autonomy and impermeability,
then male receptivity easily reads as surrender rather than relational depth.
Conversely, if femininity is tethered to nurturance and accommodation,
then agency can appear as deviation rather than integration.

Giddens argues that late modernity loosens fixed gender scripts. [^Giddens]
Yet our society does not eliminate performance pressures.
Illouz has similarly shown how romantic life has become formatted by evaluation and comparison [^Illouz].
While the old scripts may be destabilising, the grammar of control is not automatically disappearing.

Many contemporary conflicts around gender and sexuality can therefore be read
as _disputes over where control may soften and where vulnerability may remain_ a subject rather than a role.
For us, the gender turbulence we observe in our society does not signal mere confusion,
but can therefore be read as a structural renegotiation of who is permitted to receive without forfeiting legitimacy.

We do not claim that gender is reducible to control distribution,
nor that all traditions function identically.
We make a narrower observation:
gender systems have historically organised the traffic between doing and receiving.
When those systems strain or dissolve, the problem of witnessed receptivity becomes newly visible.

[^WestZimmermann: West, Candace, and Don H. Zimmerman. "Doing gender." _Gender & society 1.2 (1987): 125-151._]

## Witnessed Receptivity Defined

We have now moved across myth, development, and culture, assembling the pillars one by one.
The turn now is architectural rather than exploratory: not another image, but the span that binds them.
What follows does not introduce a new theme.
It names, in concentrated form, the structure that has been implicit all along.

**Witnessed receptivity** is the capacity to open, soften, and be affected while retaining continuity of being.
It is not simply receiving; it is receiving within a relational or internalised field that secures reversibility.
In this configuration, descent does not equal annihilation, exposure does not equal capture, and surrender does not abolish return.
The defining feature is not control, but embodied confidence that openness will not dissolve subjecthood.

Witnessed receptivity is therefore not submission, not regression, and not sacrificial self-erasure.
It does not bind the receiver into debt, hierarchy, or incorporation under a sovereign gaze.
It differs equally from defensive autonomy: armour preserves the self by contraction; stone preserves it by disappearance.
Witnessed receptivity preserves it by relation.
Nor is it identical with contemplative self-transcendence;
non-dual states may arise from it, but they do not replace its developmental ground.

The myths we have traced each articulated a necessary operator: covenant before descent,
witnessing without capture, agency without domination, re-membering after fragmentation,
and the ever-present temptation of crisis-logic.
Witnessed receptivity is the structural integration of these elements.
It names the condition under which deep receiving becomes survivable because return is encoded as possible.

We require this concept because our dominant symbolic vocabularies are thin at precisely this point.
Autonomy, mastery, sacrifice, transcendence — these we name fluently.
Receiving without self-loss, we do not.
Where that grammar is absent, bodies oscillate between control and collapse,
and cultures mistake defensive organisation for strength.
Witnessed receptivity names the missing middle posture: openness that does not cost the self.

### Early misaligned development: the emergence of agency‑biased regulatory styles

As noted earlier (see “Action‑based adaptations”),
persistent difficulties at the threshold of receiving
may stabilise into relatively enduring regulatory styles.
A common structural pattern appears:
when sustained openness becomes destabilising, the organism reorganises around agency.
That is, when deep openness is repeatedly experienced as threatening the continuity of the self,
the system recruits or stabilises mechanisms that reduce exposure to receptivity.
As already briefly discussed,
this reorganisation may sediment over time into personality‑level traits that privilege doing over receiving.

Clinically and theoretically, these styles often include constellations such as:

- compulsive self‑reliance and pseudo‑independence (avoidant attachment patterns),
- perfectionistic performance and over‑responsibility,
- relational over‑accommodation or fawning,
- dominance‑oriented control,
- emotional numbing, and
- dissociative compartmentalisation.

While these configurations appear in varied combinations,
two broad regulatory postures frequently emerge at opposite ends of the relational field.

**1. Accommodation‑dominant posture.**

Here, control is exercised through compliance and anticipation.
Self‑reliance and perfectionism coexist with
fawning tendencies, emotional numbing, and selective dissociation.
The person remains hyper‑attuned to relational threat while detaching from their own needs.
Agency is maintained, but primarily in the service of preserving connection.

**2. Assertion‑dominant posture.**

Here, control is exercised through overt direction, boundary enforcement, or dominance.
Self‑reliance and performance are again central, but here
accompanied by reduced tolerance for vulnerability, combined with similar numbing and/or dissociation.
Agency is maintained by minimising dependency and pre‑empting exposure.

Externally, these two postures may appear opposed — compliant versus forceful.
Structurally, however, they share a common feature:
both organise _experience_ so that
the person remains primarily on the _actor_ side of relational exchange, even when appearing compliant.
Sometimes receiving, especially in its deeper forms,
may become so tightly constrained that it no longer appears as an experiential option,
as our case exemplifies.

We emphasise that insufficiently witnessed receptivity is, of course,
only as _one possible developmental pathway among others_, not as an exclusive cause.
Early environments marked by misattunement, unpredictability, coercion, or conditional care
can all contribute to similar defensive organisations (cf. Winnicott; Bion; van der Kolk).
Attachment research, developmental trauma studies,
and psychodynamic formulations all demonstrate multiple pathways toward similar patterns.

Our hypothesis is that when early receiving repeatedly destabilises rather
than stabilises the self, agency‑biased organisations become especially likely.
These organisations may later interact with — and be reinforced by — cultural grammars
that privilege control, performance, and autonomy.

The fawn response illustrates this mechanism in one concentrated form.
It can be understood within polyvagal frameworks (Porges; Dana)
as a strategy that recruits social engagement capacities to maintain proximity in unsafe contexts.
Sympathetic mobilisation toward pleasing coexists with parasympathetic dampening of one’s own need.
What begins as an adaptive response may gradually stabilise into identity.
In this way, a neurobiological adaptation to unsafe receiving
can stabilise as a socially legible form of competence.

### Developmental arc: from external witness to structural reversibility

The structure of witnessed receptivity unfolds developmentally.
There is a small risk of it being misunderstood, if “witnessing” is imagined as
a permanent internal observer that must remain active during deep surrender.
This is **not** our claim.

To clarify the developmental trajectory, we distinguish several stages:

- **Stage 1: External witnessing**
  - Receptivity is stabilised through the reliable presence of an actual physical other.
  - Co-regulation precedes self-regulation.
  - Descent (into affect, intimacy, bodily openness) is survivable because return is relationally secured.

- **Stage 2: Internalisation of reversibility**
  - What becomes internalised is not an observing ego or any other conscious part/function,
    but a structural/neural confidence that collapse does not equal annihilation.
  - The nervous system encodes that intense states can be entered and exited without loss of continuity.
  - Return becomes increasingly automatic, similar to how breathing resumes after a held exhalation (bahya kumbhaka).
  - No active external or internal “watcher” is required in the moment of deep surrender;
    what is required is embodied confidence in return.

- **Stage 3 (possible but not foundational): Non-subjective awareness**
  - In some contemplative traditions and advanced experiential states,
    experience may occur without a clearly felt subject–object structure
    (“experience without experiencer”).
  - Such states are phenomenologically distinct from dissociation or collapse.
  - However, they presuppose an already integrated system capable of reversibility.
  - Non-dual or non-subjective awareness does not replace developmental witnessing;
    it emerges from a system in which receptivity has already become survivable.

This distinction guards against two confusions:

- Confusing trauma-related dissociation with contemplative self-transcendence.
- Reintroducing subtle control in the form of an internal monitor that prevents full surrender.

For the purposes of this essay, the central claim remains developmentally grounded:

> Deep receiving becomes safe not because someone remains in control,
> but because the system has learned that openness does not lead to annihilation.

Whether the non-subjective witnessing described in contemplative traditions
should be treated as a further stage of integration,
a different ontological claim,
or a distinct phenomenological domain,
is left open for later exploration and remains beyond the scope of the present essay.

### Symbolic memory and cultural thinness

The myths traced earlier will now be use as a diagnostic register:
compressed narrative forms that both preserve fail to preserve
the operators required for _reversible descent_:

- Covenant before descent (Inanna)
- Hearing without capture (Avalokiteśvara)
- Agency without dominance (Tārā)
- Re-membering (Isis, Lemminkäinen)
- Crisis logic (Seth)

Myth certainly do not _cause_ the developmental–cultural configuration we describe.
Their role is weaker and more structural:
myths and related symbolic practices shape what a culture can readily _name_, legitimise, and train.
Where the symbolic vocabulary for “receiving while remaining a subject” is robust,
reversibility becomes thinkable and discussable;
where the vocabulary is thin or coded primarily through subordination,
bodies must regulate without a shared public grammar.
The result is not only individual symptoms
but also a drift toward agency-biased solutions that feel _self-evident._
That is, we propose a mutually stabilising configuration across nervous-system regulation,
personality-level regulatory styles, and cultural grammars of control.

If so desired, the mythic record may be read retrospectively as a partial and uneven trace:
The structure shows which operators were sometimes available
and how they have been gradually eclipsed when crisis-logic has become ambient.

## A Cultural Gap

We are now ready for our central hypothesis.

We represent our hypothesis in a form of a causal configuration
where different neural, psychological, behavioural, and cultural aspects form
a self-recinforcing loop that createas stable neurosociopsychological configuration.
We present this as a stabilising configuration,
not as a single causal chain nor as an exhaustive account of Western development.

In particular, we surmise the following: For many Western cultures, there is a stable configuration where:

> there is a non-trivial proportion of individuals
> that have a neuropsychological structure
> that effectively blocks the ability for deep receiving.

This configuration is stable because of the following self-enforcing causal loop:

1. Cultural-symbolic order privileges control and symbolise only thinly (if at all) receiving without self-loss.
2. This biases early caregiving practices toward regulation-through-control, e.g.
   through institutional norms, economic pressures, and internalised ideals of autonomy.
3. Which increases likelihood of _insufficiently witnessed receiving._
4. Which increases likelihood of agency-biased regulatory styles in adults.
5. Aggregated, such styles render control-heavy cultural forms psychologically coherent and attractive.
6. Which further stabilises control-oriented symbolic orders.

As a result of that, gradually the concept of witnessed receptivity has become marginal, privatised, and thinly articulated.
In other words, if witnessed receptivity requires the internalisation of reversibility,
then cultures that fail to symbolise or train such reversibility may produce defensive alternatives at scale.

As a result of this self-reinforcing loop,
certain political-symbolic orders stabilise at privilegeing control, performance, and crisis responsiveness.
These conditions narrow the tolerance for vulnerability in early relational environments.
When receptivity is repeatedly destabilised in development,
defensive neuropsychological organisations around control intensify.
Aggregated across many individuals,
such organisations render control-heavy political forms psychologically coherent and attractive.

It is important to note that we do not claim that
the loop itself does not cause such political orders per se,
but that the loop helps such orders to stabilise if they appear.

Furhermore, we surmise that due to this cultural shortcoming,
the majority of the people who have such a forementioned structure cannot imagine
that they or any people could be able to deeply receive.
That is, they have difficulty

a) imagining or recognising deep receiving as a viable mode of being, or
b) considering that they could have such a shortcoming in their worldview.

Summarising almost poetically,

When witnessed receptivity is unsafe,
action becomes the primary regulator.
When action becomes the primary regulator,
control logics feel rational.
When control logics dominate,
structures emerge that stigmatise receptivity.
When receptivity is stigmatised,
upbringing makes deep receiving increasingly unsafe.
