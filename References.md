## References

Zoldbrod (2014), “Sexual Issues in Treating Trauma Survivors” (peer-reviewed, clinical). Covers how sexual trauma commonly expresses as sexual dysfunctions, including dissociation/numbing and anxiety/panic reactions around sexual contact and arousal, and frames these as trauma sequelae rather than “sexual problems” in isolation.  ￼

Friedman (1994), “Psychodynamic group therapy for male …” (peer-reviewed; JSTOR access). Explicitly lists, for male incest survivors, patterns including sex while dissociated, genital anaesthesia, and acute anxiety reactions when triggered—very close to your described triad (numbing → functioning → panic when defences lift).  ￼

Stappenbeck et al. (2016), “In-The-Moment Dissociation, Emotional Numbing, and …” (peer-reviewed; PMC). Focuses on in-the-moment dissociation/numbing during sexual situations and how it relates to assault history and trauma symptoms. It’s not “genital anaesthesia” specific, but it is directly on the dissociation/numbing-during-sex mechanism.  ￼

Coxell & King (2010), “Adult male rape and sexual assault … tonic immobility” (peer-reviewed; indexed in secondary reviews). This is about the freeze/tonic-immobility defensive response in male sexual assault contexts—relevant because your case reads like a precise defensive switch when “receiving” breaks through numbness.  ￼

Stirling et al. (2025), “Trauma-informed management … in pelvic health physiotherapy” (peer-reviewed; 2025 discussion paper with case study). Not sex therapy, but directly addresses sexual-trauma sequelae in pelvic contexts and is one of the few newer clinical pieces that tries to operationalise trauma-informed handling of pelvic/sexual triggers.

Amelia H. Kaplan (2006), dissertation with transcript-style clinical case material (Hakomi / body-centred psychotherapy).
Contains a detailed segment explicitly describing pendulation between “nourishment/healing vortex” and “panic/trauma vortex”, framed as the dilemma: “can [the client] remain in contact with bodily experience and still stay in relationship”. That is essentially a ventral-threshold problem in plain language.  ￼

Peter Levine interview (Psychotherapy.net): vignette where “relaxation” precipitates terror (“I’m dying”), followed by mobilisation and tremoring.
This is not sexuality-specific, but it’s a very clear first-person clinical narrative of the paradox: moving toward settling triggers alarm/collapse. It’s a readable illustration of the “doorway” instability.
  ￼
Willms et al. (2022), Frontiers in Psychiatry: case-style narrative (“medical student types journals during ketamine infusions”) describing somatic resourcing and improved access to ventral regulation.
Not sexuality-specific, but it is a concrete case narrative where the intervention focus is precisely “accessing ventral safely” via sensory cues and resourcing—i.e., widening the threshold zone.

Visco-Comandini et al. (2025), “The Body as a Vessel for Trauma: The Clinical Case Study …” (indexed on PMC but inaccessible to open here).
The abstract/summary indicates an explicit case study emphasising somatic resources and “window of tolerance” work for oscillations between hypo/hyperarousal—again, the same doorway phenomenon, in a formal case format.

Payne et al. (2015), Somatic Experiencing review on PMC (also blocked from open here).
This is not a single case report, but it’s one of the most-cited sources that lays out the mechanics of resourcing, pendulation/titration, and why pushing intensity yields panic/shutdown—i.e., how one works near the threshold.  ￼
